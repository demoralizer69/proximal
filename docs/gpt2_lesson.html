Orientation — where this paper sits

You already have GPT-1 fully internalized: pre-train a 12-layer decoder-only Transformer on BooksCorpus with next-token prediction, then fine-tune on each downstream task with input transformations and a tiny classification head. The result was SOTA on 9 of 12 benchmarks. The recipe was: pre-train + fine-tune.

This paper, written eight months later, makes a sharp turn. The recipe becomes: pre-train + prompt. No fine-tuning. No labeled data for any of the downstream tasks. The same Transformer, scaled up, is asked to do summarization, translation, question answering, reading comprehension, and language modeling on eight different benchmarks — all by formatting the input as a string of text and letting the model continue.

So GPT-2 is the pivot point. The architecture is essentially the same Transformer decoder as GPT-1; the changes are mostly stability-at-depth tweaks plus a much larger context window. What's new isn't the model — it's the thesis: that with enough data quality and enough capacity, the LM objective alone teaches enough that fine-tuning is no longer required to perform many downstream tasks.

Mental model: GPT-1 was "language modeling is a good initialization for supervised learning." GPT-2 is "language modeling is a good initialization for itself — the LM is the downstream system, and the task gets encoded into the prompt."

Why this is the seed of the scaling-laws era

The paper trains four model sizes and plots perplexity vs parameters. The curve is smooth and monotonically improving — no diminishing returns visible at the largest size they test. That single empirical observation, when later formalized by Kaplan et al. (2020) and acted on at the 175B scale (GPT-3), is the entire engine of the modern LLM industry. GPT-2 doesn't write down a scaling law, but it shows you the curve.

Part 1 — The "unsupervised multitask learner" thesis (Section 1)

The introduction is short but the title is doing heavy lifting. Let's unpack it.

The framing they explicitly attack

The dominant ML paradigm of 2018 was: collect a labeled dataset for a specific narrow task, train a model on it, deploy that model. The authors point out the failure mode:

  • Models trained this way are brittle: change the input distribution slightly and performance falls off a cliff.
  • They generalize poorly across tasks. A SQuAD model can't translate, a translation model can't summarize.
  • Even the GPT-1 / BERT recipe — pre-train then fine-tune — produces $T$ different fine-tuned models for $T$ different tasks. You still need labels for each.

Their proposal: instead of $T$ task-specific systems, train a single system that does all $T$ tasks as a side effect of one unified objective.

The "task" framing — $P(\text{output} \mid \text{input}, \text{task})$

Standard supervised learning estimates $P(\text{output} \mid \text{input})$. Multitask learning generalizes that to $P(\text{output} \mid \text{input}, \text{task})$, where the task is an explicit signal (a one-hot vector, a learned task embedding, etc.). McCann et al.'s "decaNLP" had floated the idea of specifying the task in natural language. GPT-2's claim is the next step:

Since the (input, task, output) triple is itself just a sequence of symbols, a sufficiently general language model — trained to model the joint distribution of all such symbol sequences that occur naturally — should already approximate $P(\text{output} \mid \text{input}, \text{task})$.

Doubt: what does "task" mean when there's no task supervision?

Sitting with this question is important because the title leans on it. Their answer is subtle. The "task" is not a label given to the model. It's encoded in the input format itself. Consider four sentences that might appear in WebText:

  • "The translation of 'cat' in French is chat." — embeds an English→French translation task.
  • "Q: Who wrote Hamlet? A: William Shakespeare." — embeds a QA task.
  • "... [long article] TL;DR: the new policy aims to reduce emissions ..." — embeds a summarization task.
  • "The Spanish word for water is agua." — embeds a definition task.

Each is just a string of tokens. But each contains both a task description (the phrase "translation of X in French is", or "TL;DR:") and an example output (the answer that follows). When the model learns to predict next tokens on the whole web, it implicitly learns the conditional distribution of answers given task-formatted prompts. At test time, you give it the prompt; it generates the answer because that's the most likely continuation.

So the title's claim — "language models are unsupervised multitask learners" — is the hypothesis that a sufficiently strong LM, trained on a sufficiently rich corpus, will implicitly learn many task-specific conditionals because those conditionals are literally present in the training distribution.

Doubt: is this the methodological argument or the practical one?

Both, and the paper is explicit. The methodological argument: by refusing to fine-tune, you measure the LM's intrinsic capacity. Any number you report is purely a function of pre-training, not of test-task data fitting. The practical argument: if it works, you've built one model that does everything, no labels required.

The paper leans more on the methodological argument throughout, because the zero-shot numbers are not actually competitive with fine-tuned SOTA on most tasks. The point is the trajectory: zero-shot capability emerges and grows with scale.

Mental model after Section 1:
  • Standard ML is brittle and per-task. Multitask learning offers a way out.
  • Tasks can be encoded into the input as natural-language prompts. So the multitask distribution $P(y \mid x, \text{task})$ is a marginal of $P(\text{text})$.
  • A good language model trained on natural text should therefore already do tasks zero-shot — no labels, no fine-tuning. The paper sets out to show that this happens at sufficient scale.

Part 2 — The Approach (Section 2)

Section 2 is divided into three parts: the corpus (2.1 WebText), the tokenizer (2.2 Input representation), and the model (2.3). I'll cover each — but the corpus and tokenizer get disproportionate attention because that's where most of the GPT-2 → GPT-1 delta lives.

Part 2.1 — WebText: building a quality corpus

GPT-1 used BooksCorpus (~800M words, ~5 GB). GPT-2 needs more data and data that contains task-like structure in its natural form (translations, QA pairs, summaries, etc.). Books are good for narrative but light on task-formatted text. So the authors construct a new corpus, WebText. The construction recipe matters.

The construction pipeline

Reddit (all outbound links, Dec 2017) │ ▼ Filter: only links from posts with karma ≥ 3 │ (a human-curation signal) ▼ Scrape the linked web pages (~45 million links) │ ▼ Extract main text with Dragnet + Newspaper libraries │ ▼ Deduplicate (near-duplicate documents removed) │ ▼ Heuristic cleanup │ ▼ Drop all Wikipedia pages │ ▼ Final corpus: ~8 million documents, ~40 GB of text

Doubt: why scrape Reddit outbound links specifically?

This is one of the most interesting design choices in the paper, and worth sitting with. The alternatives the authors considered (and the field used) were:

  • Common Crawl — a snapshot of much of the web. Huge (terabytes), but the quality is famously poor. Lots of SEO spam, machine-generated content, navigation menus, cookie banners, etc. Earlier work (Trinh & Le 2018) reported that raw Common Crawl was almost unusable for LM pre-training.
  • More books — books are clean but there are only so many. And books underrepresent the task-formatted text the authors want.
  • Curated lists like Wikipedia — clean but stylistically narrow. And Wikipedia is used as a test set for many of the LM benchmarks they want to evaluate on.

The Reddit-outbound-with-karma trick threads the needle:

What the karma filter is implicitly doing: a Reddit submission gets karma when humans upvote it. So a link with karma ≥ 3 is one where multiple humans saw the linked page, judged it worth reading, and signaled that judgment. The filter is, effectively, a distributed human quality rating — far cheaper than paying annotators, far more diverse than a curated list, and applied at web scale.

You're not getting the median web page; you're getting pages that humans actively chose to share. The result skews toward articles, essays, tutorials, news, technical writeups, and discussion threads — exactly the kind of structured text that contains task-like patterns.

Doubt: why the threshold of 3 specifically?

The authors don't ablate the threshold. Three is "low enough to keep volume up" — Reddit's karma distribution is heavily right-skewed, so 3 is barely above noise. But it filters out spam links posted to dead subreddits, automated bot posts, etc. Higher thresholds (say 100) would collapse the dataset to a few popular subreddits and skew it stylistically. Three is the lowest meaningful signal.

Doubt: why exclude Wikipedia?

This is a methodological decision. Several of the LM benchmarks they'll evaluate zero-shot — WikiText-103, WikiText-2 — are derived from Wikipedia. If WebText contained Wikipedia text, the test set would partially overlap with the training set, inflating perplexity numbers via memorization rather than generalization. By dropping Wikipedia entirely, they make the zero-shot evaluation honest.

(This is the same concern that motivates Section 4's 8-gram bloom filter analysis. The authors take train-test contamination seriously throughout the paper.)

Doubt: why not just use much more BooksCorpus?

BooksCorpus is what BooksCorpus is — there isn't a 10× version sitting around. More importantly, books contain very little task-formatted text. There's no "TL;DR:" in chapter 3 of a novel. No "Q: ... A: ...". No interlinear translations. To get zero-shot performance on summarization, translation, and QA, you need a corpus where those patterns actually occur. The open web has all of them.

The result

WebText final stats:
  • ~8 million documents
  • ~40 GB of cleaned plain text
  • ~8× larger (by byte count) than BooksCorpus
  • Stylistically diverse (essays, news, technical writing, fan content, forums)
  • Contains naturally-occurring task patterns (TL;DRs, translations, Q&As)
  • No Wikipedia, no GPT-2's own Reddit posts (just outbound links)
The corpus is the experiment. The paper isn't really claiming "Transformers can do zero-shot tasks." It's claiming "Transformers trained on a corpus that contains task patterns can do zero-shot tasks." Pre-training data quality is doing a lot of the work.

Part 2.2 — Input representation: byte-level BPE

GPT-1 used standard BPE with a 40k vocabulary. GPT-2 uses byte-level BPE with a 50,257-token vocabulary. The change looks small. It isn't.

What standard BPE does, briefly

BPE (byte-pair encoding) starts from a base alphabet (e.g. Unicode characters) and iteratively merges the most-frequent adjacent pair into a new symbol. After $N$ merges, you have a vocabulary of $|\text{base}| + N$ tokens — the original characters plus $N$ multi-character "subword" units. Common substrings ("ing", "tion", "the") become single tokens; rare words decompose into character-like pieces. The result is open-vocabulary (any string can be encoded) and efficient (common stuff is one token).

The problem with character-level BPE on the open web

If your base alphabet is Unicode characters, you have ~143,000 base symbols just to cover every character that might appear on the web (emoji, Chinese, Cyrillic, mathematical operators, control characters, ...). The embedding table for the base alone is huge, and most of those base tokens get almost zero training signal because they appear maybe once in gigabytes of text.

You'd like a small fixed base alphabet that's guaranteed to cover every possible input — but Unicode is too big.

The byte-level trick

Bytes are the smallest unit. There are exactly 256 of them. Every string, in any language, ultimately encodes as a sequence of UTF-8 bytes. So if you make bytes your base alphabet:

  • The base is small (256) and fully covered with no rare-symbol problem.
  • The vocabulary is closed: every possible input string is representable, including emoji, code, non-Latin scripts, garbled HTML.
  • No <UNK> token is ever needed.

Then you run BPE merges on top of bytes. The merges will mostly produce ASCII subword units (because English dominates the corpus), but byte-level multi-byte sequences for non-Latin characters get represented faithfully too. With 50,000 merges plus the 256 base bytes plus a single end-of-text token, you land on a vocabulary of exactly 50,257.

Doubt: doesn't this make non-Latin text use way more tokens per character?

Yes — a Chinese character, which is one Unicode character, becomes 3 UTF-8 bytes and therefore up to 3 GPT-2 tokens. For an English-dominated corpus and English-dominated evaluations, that's a fine trade: English is efficient, other languages are inefficient but representable. For a paper whose training data is mostly English, this is the right optimization.

Doubt: GPT-1 was already BPE — so what's the actual delta?

Two things:

  1. Closed vocabulary, no UNK. GPT-1 had occasional unknown-token issues. GPT-2 never does — every byte sequence is representable.
  2. One regularity: prevent BPE from merging across character categories. The paper explicitly notes that they prevent merges that span byte ranges from different categories (letter/digit/punctuation/whitespace), with one exception for whitespace. This avoids weird tokens like "dog." being one symbol while "dog!" is another; tokenization stays predictable.
Why this matters downstream: A robust, closed-vocabulary tokenizer is a prerequisite for the "tasks live inside the input" thesis. If translation requires the model to emit French, and French uses accented characters, those characters need to be representable. Byte-level BPE guarantees this. The same applies to code, math, formatting, URLs — anything you might want a future LLM to handle ends up just working.

Part 2.3 — The model: what changed from GPT-1

Architecturally, GPT-2 is the same decoder-only Transformer as GPT-1. There are four specific changes the paper calls out, plus a context-length increase and a vocab-size increase. All five are stability/scale fixes, not capability changes.

Change 1 — Pre-LayerNorm (the most important change)

In the original Vaswani Transformer and GPT-1, LayerNorm sits after each sub-layer's residual addition: this is "post-LN." The block looks like:

$h' = \text{LayerNorm}(h + \text{Attn}(h)),\quad h'' = \text{LayerNorm}(h' + \text{FFN}(h')).$

GPT-2 moves LayerNorm to before each sub-layer, with the residual addition coming after, unnormalized: this is "pre-LN."

$h' = h + \text{Attn}(\text{LayerNorm}(h)),\quad h'' = h' + \text{FFN}(\text{LayerNorm}(h')).$
POST-LN (GPT-1, Vaswani) PRE-LN (GPT-2) x x │ │ ├──────────┐ ├──────────┐ │ Attention │ LayerNorm │ │ │ │ │ ▼ │ Attention │ (add) ◄──────────── │ │ │ │ │ ▼ │ LayerNorm └─────► (add) │ │ │ ▼ ▼ ▼ (same pattern for FFN) (LN inside, residual outside)

Why pre-LN matters specifically at depth

Consider the gradient flow through a stack of $L$ blocks. The residual connection $h_{\ell+1} = h_\ell + f(h_\ell)$ means that, ignoring the function $f$, the identity gradient flows uninterrupted from layer $L$ back to layer $0$. This is exactly why residual networks train at depth in the first place.

But in post-LN, the LayerNorm sits on the residual stream itself: every layer's output passes through a LayerNorm, which is not the identity. So the gradient at layer $\ell$ gets multiplied by the Jacobian of LayerNorm $L - \ell$ times before reaching the embedding. LayerNorm is well-conditioned but not a pure identity, and the accumulated rescaling causes either gradient explosion or vanishing as $L$ grows. Empirically this is why post-LN Transformers need careful learning-rate warmup and become unstable past ~12 layers without tricks.

In pre-LN, the residual stream is untouched: $h_{\ell+1} = h_\ell + f(\text{LayerNorm}(h_\ell))$. The identity path from input to output is genuinely an identity. The LayerNorm only normalizes the input to $f$, not the residual itself. Gradients flow through the residual stream essentially unchanged, and the network trains stably at arbitrary depth.

Pre-LN is what makes the 48-layer, 1.5B-parameter GPT-2 trainable without the heroics that post-LN at that depth would require. It's the single most important architectural change.

Doubt: why didn't the original Transformer use pre-LN?

At Vaswani's depth (6 encoder + 6 decoder layers) post-LN worked fine with a warmup schedule. The instability only matters at the depths GPT-2 explores. Modern Transformers essentially all use pre-LN, and that convention starts here.

Change 2 — Residual weight scaling by $1/\sqrt{N}$

At initialization, every block adds something to the residual stream. After $N$ blocks, the residual stream has accumulated $N$ contributions. If each is initialized from $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, the variance of the residual stream grows as $N \sigma^2$, which blows up at depth.

Where exactly does the $1/\sqrt{N}$ go?

This is the part the paper is terse about. Each Transformer block has two sub-layers, each ending with a projection that writes back to the residual stream:

  • In the attention sub-layer: the output projection $W_O \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ that mixes the per-head outputs back to $d_{\text{model}}$.
  • In the FFN sub-layer: the second linear $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{4d \times d}$ that projects from the $4d$ hidden back down to $d_{\text{model}}$.

These are the two "exit" weights — the very last linear ops before the value gets added into the residual stream. Those are the matrices whose initialization gets divided by $\sqrt{N}$. Every other weight in the block ($W_Q, W_K, W_V$, the FFN up-projection $W_1$, embeddings) keeps its standard init.

The intuition for why these two weights specifically: only the exit projection controls how much each sub-layer contributes to the residual stream. The query, key, value, and up-projection weights affect the internal computation of the sub-layer, but their effect on the residual stream is mediated through $W_O$ / $W_2$. Scaling those two matrices is the most direct lever on "how much does this block change $h$?"

The variance accumulation, made explicit

Let $f_\ell$ be the function the $\ell$-th block adds to the residual stream: $h_{\ell+1} = h_\ell + f_\ell(h_\ell)$. Unroll across all $N$ blocks:

$h_N \;=\; h_0 \;+\; \sum_{\ell=1}^{N} f_\ell(h_\ell).$

Each $f_\ell$ ends in either $W_O$ or $W_2$ (and the block has two of these summed in, but treat $N$ as counting all residual additions — so $N = 2 \times n_{\text{layers}}$ in the actual code). Now compare with vs without the scaling. Let "standard init" mean variance $\sigma^2$ per output element; assume $f_\ell$ outputs are approximately uncorrelated across $\ell$ at initialization.

SettingPer-block contribution varianceTotal variance after $N$ blocks
Without scaling$\sigma^2$$N \sigma^2 \;\to\;$ blows up with depth
With $W_O, W_2$ init × $1/\sqrt{N}$$\sigma^2 / N$ (variance scales as the squared init scale)$N \cdot \sigma^2 / N \;=\; \sigma^2 \;\to\;$ constant in $N$
$\text{Var}\!\left[\sum_{\ell=1}^{N} f_\ell(h_\ell)\right] \;=\; \sum_{\ell=1}^{N} \text{Var}[f_\ell] \;\approx\; N \cdot \frac{\sigma^2}{N} \;=\; \sigma^2.$

The first equality uses independence of the $f_\ell$ contributions at init (sums of independent variances). The second substitutes the scaled-init contribution variance.

Why dividing the init (not the activation directly) achieves this

A linear layer's output variance scales as $\|W\|^2$ times the input variance. If you initialize $W$ from $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_W^2 / N)$ instead of $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_W^2)$, the output variance shrinks by a factor of $N$ — equivalent to "the block contributes $1/N$ as much variance to the residual stream." That's why the $1/\sqrt{N}$ goes on the standard deviation of the init (square it for variance).

Mental model: pre-LN keeps the residual stream un-normalized between blocks, so its variance is free to grow. To stop it growing, GPT-2 just makes each block's exit projection start out small enough that the sum across all $N$ blocks lands at $\sigma^2$ instead of $N \sigma^2$.

This is a small, principled change that combines with pre-LN to keep activations well-scaled at initialization regardless of depth. Without it, the larger models would either saturate or explode in the first few steps.

Change 3 — An extra LayerNorm after the final block

Because pre-LN leaves the residual stream un-normalized between blocks, the activations flowing out of the last block aren't normalized either. GPT-2 adds a single LayerNorm after the final block (before the output projection to logits) to normalize the final hidden state. This is a one-liner, but it's the natural completion of the pre-LN choice.

Change 4 — Context length doubled to 1024

GPT-1 used a context window of 512 tokens. GPT-2 uses 1024. This costs 4× the attention compute (attention is quadratic in sequence length, so doubling the window quadruples the per-block attention cost), but gives the model meaningfully more context to exploit. For long-document tasks like CoQA and summarization, this matters.

Change 5 — Vocabulary 50,257 (vs 40,000)

Direct consequence of byte-level BPE, covered above. Slightly larger embedding tables; slightly more output classes to softmax over. Negligible compute cost.

Change 6 (implicit) — Batch size 512

The paper trains with a batch size of 512 sequences (of 1024 tokens each), so each optimizer step sees ~524k tokens. This is much larger than GPT-1's batches and is part of the scaling story.

Doubt: why scale specifically to 1.5B? Is there a principled reason or pure engineering?

This is mostly engineering, with one principled choice on top. The principled part: having decided to study how zero-shot capability scales with model size, you need a wide range of model sizes to see the trend. So they build 4 models spanning ~13× in parameters. The 117M and 345M sizes anchor against GPT-1 and BERT-Large respectively; the 762M and 1542M sizes extend into territory nobody had trained before.

The engineering part: 1.5B parameters at fp32, with optimizer state, is about 24 GB of memory — feasible on a small TPU or GPU cluster of the era. The authors didn't go higher because they ran out of practical memory headroom on their training setup. GPT-3 will go ~115× bigger because the infrastructure catches up.

So 1.5B isn't a magical number. It's the biggest the authors could fit. The interesting fact is that even at 1.5B, the perplexity curve hadn't bottomed out — which is the empirical seed of "scaling laws say we should keep going."

Doubt: why drop fine-tuning entirely?

Three interleaved reasons, in increasing order of how strongly the paper leans on them:

  1. Methodological purity. Zero-shot is the cleanest measurement of LM capability. Any number you report is purely about pre-training, not about test-task data fitting. This lets you make claims about scaling pre-training rather than scaling fine-tuning.
  2. Multitask claim. If the thesis is "a sufficiently good LM does many tasks," then evaluating fine-tuned-per-task would invalidate the claim — you'd be showing one model trained for each task, not one model doing all tasks.
  3. Forward-looking. Fine-tuning requires labeled data and per-task training infrastructure. Prompting requires neither. If you can show prompting works, you've described a paradigm that is dramatically more usable in practice. (This foreshadows GPT-3 and ChatGPT.)

Note what they're not saying: they're not saying fine-tuning is bad. The appendix and later work confirm GPT-2 fine-tuned beats zero-shot on every task. The methodological choice is "this paper is about zero-shot," not "fine-tuning has been deprecated."

Part 3 — Zero-shot Experiments (Section 3)

This is the body of the paper. Eight task domains, each evaluated zero-shot. For each domain, two things matter: (a) the result, and (b) the prompting mechanism — how the authors phrase the task so a vanilla LM can attempt it.

The unifying trick across all of these: the model is never told what task to do. It is given a string and asked to continue, just like during pre-training. The "task signal" is buried in the format of the string. If the model has seen enough strings of that format during pre-training, it will continue in a way that constitutes solving the task.

Part 3.1 — Language modeling benchmarks

The first set of evaluations is the most natural: how well does the LM model text from distributions it didn't train on? Eight LM benchmarks:

What are perplexity and bits-per-character?

Both are direct functions of the same thing: the average log-loss the model achieves on the test corpus.

Perplexity is the exponential of the per-token cross-entropy:

$$\text{PPL} \;=\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\log P(u_i \mid u_{\lt i})\right).$$

Intuitively, perplexity is "the effective branching factor": a perplexity of 30 means the model is, on average, as uncertain about the next token as if there were 30 equiprobable choices. Lower is better.

Bits-per-character converts the same quantity into bits and normalizes by characters instead of tokens (so it's directly comparable across tokenization schemes):

$$\text{BPC} \;=\; \frac{1}{N_{\text{chars}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N}-\log_2 P(u_i \mid u_{\lt i}).$$

For enwik8, GPT-2 achieves about 0.93 BPC, beating the prior SOTA of ~1.05. For WikiText-103, perplexity drops from ~18 (prior SOTA) to ~17.5.

Doubt: why does GPT-2 do well on these despite never training on them?

Two reasons:

  • The benchmarks measure how well the model predicts generic English text. WebText contains plenty of generic English text, including content stylistically similar to news articles (Penn Treebank), curated articles (WikiText), and prose (CBT). Performance transfers.
  • Crucially, the authors excluded Wikipedia from WebText. So the WikiText benchmarks aren't testing memorization — they're testing genuine LM transfer.

Doubt: why does GPT-2 not beat SOTA on 1 Billion Word?

The 1 Billion Word Benchmark is a sentence-shuffled corpus. Sentences within each document are randomly permuted. So the long-range structure that GPT-2's 1024-token context is built to exploit doesn't exist in that test distribution. The model is forced to do isolated-sentence prediction, where smaller models trained directly on shuffled data have an advantage. This is a clean illustration of the BooksCorpus argument from GPT-1: the corpus shapes what the model learns, and benchmarks that destroy structure don't reward models that learn structure.

Doubt: what does this perplexity improvement tell us about generalization vs memorization?

The fact that perplexity improves on benchmarks GPT-2 has never seen (and whose sources were specifically excluded from WebText) is direct evidence that pre-training is producing generalizable language modeling capability, not just memorization of training data. The bloom-filter analysis in Section 4 reinforces this by measuring n-gram overlap directly.

Part 3.2 — CBT and LAMBADA: the mechanics of prompting

Children's Book Test (CBT)

CBT presents a passage of children's literature with one word removed, plus a list of 10 candidate words. The task: pick the correct word.

Prompting mechanism: for each candidate $c$, substitute it into the blank, then compute $P(\text{full passage with } c) = \prod_i P(u_i \mid u_{\lt i})$ under the LM. Pick the candidate with highest probability.

Passage: "The cat sat on the ____ and watched the rain fall." Candidates: [mat, dog, sky, table, cloud, ...] For each candidate c: reconstructed = "The cat sat on the " + c + " and watched the rain fall." score(c) = sum_i log P(token_i | token_{<i}) under the LM Pick argmax_c score(c).

Notice: the model isn't told "this is a multiple-choice task." It scores each completion by language-model likelihood, and we pick the best. The model's preference over plausible continuations is the answer.

CBT comes in two versions: CBT-CN (common nouns, what we just described) and CBT-NE (named entities, where the blank is a character name and the candidates are characters from the book). GPT-2 reaches ~93.3% on CBT-CN and ~89.1% on CBT-NE at the 1.5B size — both new SOTA.

LAMBADA

LAMBADA is harder. Each example is a passage of ~5 sentences, with the final word removed. The passages are specifically selected so that the final word is unpredictable from the last sentence alone but predictable given the full context. So you can't cheat by looking only at local syntax — you have to track long-range entities.

Example structure:

"She had been thinking about that day for weeks. Her grandmother had told her stories about it. The painting depicted a young girl, painted by her great-aunt who had lived in Paris during the war. The girl was holding a small bouquet of ____." The final word ("flowers", "violets", whichever the original was) is guessable only because the passage set up "bouquet" context. A local bigram model would have no chance.

Prompting: simply compute $P(w \mid \text{passage})$ under the LM for the candidate word $w$. Or, in accuracy mode, take the most likely next-word completion and check whether it matches the target.

The "stop-word filter" trick

One subtle issue: a naive LM will often predict common words ("the", "and", ".") rather than committing to a content word. The authors find that filtering out stop-word predictions (restricting to tokens that could plausibly end a sentence) improves LAMBADA accuracy substantially. This is one of the few task-specific tweaks in the paper, and it's worth noting as an honest disclosure that pure zero-shot is sometimes brittle.

GPT-2 1.5B reaches ~63.2% accuracy on LAMBADA, vs prior SOTA of ~59.2%. The perplexity metric (~8.6) is also new SOTA, dropping from ~99 (yes, two-orders-of-magnitude improvement on this metric — it's that hard for older models).

Mechanism summary for LM benchmarks (CBT, LAMBADA, perplexity tasks):
  • No special prompt — feed the passage in directly.
  • The "task" is encoded by what gets scored: either a probability over a fixed last token (LAMBADA), or a comparison of likelihoods across candidates (CBT), or a sum of token log-probs over the whole test set (perplexity).
  • The model is just being asked "how likely is this text?" The task framing happens outside the model.

Part 3.3 — Winograd Schema Challenge

The Winograd Schema is a commonsense-reasoning benchmark. Each example is a sentence with an ambiguous pronoun and two possible referents:

"The trophy doesn't fit in the brown suitcase because it is too large. What does it refer to? (a) the trophy (b) the suitcase"

Prompting: substitute each referent for the pronoun, and pick the substitution that yields a higher LM probability.

Sentence A: "The trophy doesn't fit in the brown suitcase because the trophy is too large." Sentence B: "The trophy doesn't fit in the brown suitcase because the suitcase is too large." score_A = sum_i log P(token_i in A | prefix) score_B = sum_i log P(token_i in B | prefix) Predict the referent corresponding to whichever sentence the LM finds more probable.

The intuition: if the model has learned commonsense ("trophies that are too large don't fit in suitcases"), the trophy-substitution should produce a more coherent sentence and therefore higher likelihood. GPT-2 reaches ~70.7% on Winograd, a clear improvement over the ~63.7% prior SOTA — but still well below human (~92%).

Part 3.4 — Reading comprehension (CoQA)

CoQA is a conversational QA benchmark: you're given a document plus a multi-turn dialogue of questions about it. Each new question may refer back to previous questions ("Who was the first president?" → "George Washington." → "When was he born?").

Prompting: format the entire history as a string, then ask the model to continue with the next answer.

[document text] Q: Who was the first president? A: George Washington. Q: When was he born? A: ← model continues here Greedy decoding from the model continues with "1732", which is the correct answer.

The key prompting elements:

  • The Q: and A: markers tell the model "this is a Q&A format" — those exact tokens occur frequently in WebText (interviews, FAQ pages, Q&A forums).
  • Listing prior Q-A turns gives the model in-context examples of how to answer — a kind of accidental few-shot prompting before few-shot was a named concept.
  • The trailing A: primes the model to emit an answer next.

GPT-2 zero-shot reaches an F1 of ~55 on CoQA's dev set, compared to ~89 for a BERT-based supervised system. The gap is large — but the supervised system was fine-tuned on 127k labeled QA pairs, and GPT-2 used zero. The fact that zero-shot reaches more than half of supervised performance via prompt formatting alone is the striking result.

Part 3.5 — Summarization (the "TL;DR:" trick)

Summarization is the most famous prompting trick in the paper. Given a news article, the authors append the literal string "TL;DR:" and ask the model to continue.

[article text, possibly thousands of tokens long] TL;DR: ← model continues with what it believes is a summary Decode 100 tokens via top-k sampling (k=2). The output is taken as the summary.

Why this works

"TL;DR" — "too long; didn't read" — is an internet convention. Bloggers, journalists, and Reddit users routinely place a short summary after a "TL;DR:" tag at the end of a long post. In WebText, every "TL;DR:" token is followed by a human-written summary of preceding content. The model has therefore learned, as part of next-token prediction, the conditional distribution $P(\text{summary} \mid \text{article} + \text{"TL;DR:"})$.

The benchmark used is CNN/Daily Mail. Quantitative results: GPT-2 zero-shot scores ROUGE-L of ~26.6, which is below specialized supervised models (~37+) but well above a random-3-sentence baseline (~24) and above a "first-3-sentences" lead baseline (~22.7).

Doubt: how do we know the "TL;DR" really matters?

The authors ablate this. Without the "TL;DR:" tag, performance drops by a few ROUGE points to roughly the lead-3 baseline. With it, performance is meaningfully higher. This is direct evidence that the format-as-task-encoding thesis holds: the model isn't summarizing because it's been "told to" in any direct sense — it's summarizing because the prompt induces a context in which summarization is the most likely continuation.

"TL;DR:" is doing a lot of work. The model has effectively been pre-trained on millions of TL;DR pairs. The "task" is fully encoded by four ASCII characters.

Part 3.6 — Translation (the bilingual prompt trick)

This is the most surprising experiment in the paper. WebText was filtered to be mostly English. There was no explicit parallel-corpus collection. And yet GPT-2 can translate, in a limited way, between English and French.

The prompt format

For English→French, the authors construct a prompt that looks like a bilingual dictionary entry:

"english sentence 1" = "french sentence 1" "english sentence 2" = "french sentence 2" ... "english sentence k" = "french sentence k" "input english sentence" = ← model continues here The continuation is taken as the French translation.

(The authors actually use a slightly different format with the words "translates to" or similar — but the essence is the same: examples of the form $\langle$English$\rangle$ = $\langle$French$\rangle$ followed by an English input.)

Note this is technically few-shot in the sense of using $k$ exemplar pairs in context, but the model was never trained on labeled translation pairs. From the LM's perspective, the prompt is just a continuation problem: "I see a pattern of English = French lines; what comes next?"

The numbers

GPT-2 reaches ~5 BLEU on WMT-14 En→Fr. For comparison: dedicated supervised systems were already at ~33+ BLEU. So GPT-2 is bad at translation in an absolute sense. But it's not zero, and it's better than a word-by-word unsupervised baseline.

Doubt: where does the French come from if WebText is mostly English?

This is the right question. Three explanations, all real:

  1. WebText isn't 100% English. The filtering was "loose" — pages with primarily English content but some French were included. The authors estimate ~10 MB of French text snuck in via mixed-language pages, language-learning sites, code-switched forum posts, etc. That's tiny compared to a real translation corpus, but it's nonzero.
  2. WebText contains "bilingual context" in the wild. Pages that discuss French words, French-themed travel content, Wikipedia-style "the French translation of X is Y" patterns (Wikipedia was excluded but Wikipedia-style content from other sources wasn't), Reddit threads about French translations, language-learning resources, etc. The model sees enough English-French co-occurrence to associate vocabulary across the two languages.
  3. The prompt amplifies the signal. By using $k$ exemplar pairs in context, the model is reminded what task structure it should be operating in. The in-context examples constrain the output distribution far more than a single English input would.

So translation works at all because (a) the training data contains a small but nonzero amount of bilingual signal, and (b) the prompt format extracts that signal effectively. The result is poor by translation-system standards but striking by "this model never saw a translation dataset" standards. It is concrete evidence that capability can emerge from content the data accidentally contains.

Part 3.7 — Question answering (Natural Questions)

Natural Questions tests open-domain factoid QA: given a question with no context document, produce the answer.

Prompting: $k$ example QA pairs in context, then the test question with a trailing A:.

Q: When was Marie Curie born? A: 7 November 1867. Q: What is the capital of Australia? A: Canberra. ... Q: [test question] A: ← model continues here

The result is striking in the opposite direction from translation: GPT-2 1.5B reaches only ~4.1% accuracy on the most popular slice of Natural Questions. Supervised systems were at ~30%+. So the model has clearly memorized some factual knowledge from WebText, but its factoid recall is unreliable.

What this tells us

The pattern across all the zero-shot tasks is: prompting works partially, but the gap to supervised SOTA varies enormously by task. Tasks closer to language modeling itself (LAMBADA, CBT, perplexity) reach SOTA. Tasks requiring narrow factual recall (NQ) are far below. Tasks requiring world knowledge plus light generation (summarization, Winograd) are in between.

Part 4 — Generalization vs Memorization

A natural objection to the zero-shot results: maybe the model isn't generalizing, it's just memorizing? WebText was scraped from the open web, which contains lots of text, including possibly contaminating snippets of the test sets. If the test sets are leaking into training, the perplexity improvements are inflated and don't reflect real generalization.

The authors take this seriously and run an 8-gram overlap analysis using a Bloom filter.

The mechanism, step by step

  1. Build a Bloom filter containing every 8-gram (8 consecutive tokens) from WebText. Bloom filters give probabilistic membership queries: "is this 8-gram in WebText?" with a tunable false-positive rate but no false negatives.
  2. For each test corpus, compute the fraction of test 8-grams that appear in WebText. This upper-bounds the overlap.
  3. Compare overlap rates to the overlap between standard train and test splits of those same datasets, to give a baseline expectation of normal overlap.

Doubt: why is this concern bigger for GPT-2 than for GPT-1?

Three reasons:

  • WebText was scraped from the open web, where benchmark test sets often appear in some form (forum copies, mirrors, derivative works, the original web sources that the benchmark was built from). BooksCorpus is a closed collection of novels and is much less likely to contain academic test sets.
  • Zero-shot evaluation means the model is evaluated on the exact test distribution. Fine-tuned evaluation (GPT-1) means the model's behavior on the test set is mediated by task-specific fine-tuning, so even some contamination during pre-training might not directly translate into inflated test scores. Zero-shot has no such mediation.
  • Scale amplifies memorization. A 1.5B model has the capacity to memorize verbatim more of its training set than a 117M model. So memorization-as-failure-mode is a more pressing concern at scale.

This is the first paper in the GPT lineage to explicitly run contamination analysis. Every later paper (GPT-3, Llama, etc.) does the same.

What the contamination analysis really establishes: the perplexity numbers are honest. Whatever the model is doing, it isn't cheating. This is necessary infrastructure for the broader claim of the paper — without it, every result is suspect.

Part 5 — Related Work + Discussion (Sections 5–6)

Section 5 surveys related work — McCann et al.'s multitask "decaNLP" framework, ELMo, BERT, Universal Language Model Fine-tuning (ULMFiT), CoVe. The throughline they pick out: prior work either kept tasks supervised (decaNLP) or required fine-tuning (everything else). GPT-2 sits in the unique position of no labels, no fine-tuning.

Section 6 — Discussion

Three points the discussion emphasizes:

1. The scaling trend

Across all eight LM benchmarks, plotting test perplexity (log scale) against parameter count (log scale) gives a roughly straight, downward-sloping line. The 1.5B model is no worse on this curve than the trend predicts — meaning there is no visible saturation. If you keep scaling, you should keep getting better.

log-perplexity (sketch) │ │ ● 117M │ │ ● 345M │ │ ● 762M │ │ ● 1542M │ \ │ \ │ \ ← trend keeps going │ \ │ └──────────────────────────────────► log(params)

This single picture is the seed of the scaling-laws era. The paper doesn't formalize it — Kaplan et al. (2020) does that, and GPT-3 (2020) acts on it at 175B. But the empirical curve here is what motivated everything that came next.

2. Underfitting at all scales

The authors note that even the 1.5B model is underfitting WebText — held-out perplexity is still improving when training stops, the train/test gap is small. So the bottleneck at the time of writing wasn't generalization; it was that they didn't have enough compute to fully train even the biggest model on the data they had. This is a clean message: "bigger models, more compute, would help."

3. The methodological claim

The discussion is careful to repeat that zero-shot is not the goal in any practical sense — it's a measurement. The paper sets a lower bound on what a language model trained this way can do on each task. Real applications might fine-tune, prompt more carefully, or stack on other techniques. The point is that the LM itself, in its raw form, already does so much of the work that the additional task-specific machinery contributes less than people thought.

View 2 — The philosophical shift: pre-train + fine-tune → pre-train + prompt

GPT-1 / BERT paradigm: GPT-2 paradigm: pre-train ──► fine-tune for task A ──► pre-train ──► prompt for task A └──► fine-tune for task B ──► └──► prompt for task B └──► fine-tune for task C ──► └──► prompt for task C └──► prompt for task D └──► prompt for task E └──► ... (one model per task) (one model, infinitely many tasks)

Why this shift matters:

  • It changes who "uses" the model. Fine-tuning requires ML engineers with labeled data. Prompting requires anyone who can write English. The user interface of AI fundamentally changes.
  • It changes how models are deployed. One deployed model can serve arbitrary tasks. No need to maintain $T$ fine-tuned checkpoints.
  • It changes what the model is for. A fine-tuned model is a classifier for a specific task. A prompted LM is a general-purpose text continuation system that, by virtue of what humans write, accidentally does everything.
  • It changes the bottleneck. Under fine-tuning, the bottleneck is labeled data. Under prompting, the bottleneck is pre-training scale and corpus quality. These are scalable in completely different ways.
GPT-1 was about transfer learning. GPT-2 is about the LM itself becoming the product. The fine-tuning step disappears. What's left is just a really good language model and the prompts you wrap around it.

The one-sentence summary

Train a 1.5B-parameter Transformer LM on 40 GB of human-curated web text, and it will, without any task-specific fine-tuning, do reading comprehension, summarization, translation, and question answering at non-trivial levels — purely by continuing prompts whose format encodes the task — and the perplexity-vs-scale curve doesn't bend, suggesting the recipe is nowhere near its ceiling.

Honest weaknesses of the paper (in 2026 hindsight)

  • Zero-shot is well below fine-tuned SOTA on most non-LM tasks. The headline "7 of 8 LM benchmarks" hides that QA, translation, and summarization remain far behind specialized systems. The paper is honest about this; later readers sometimes aren't.
  • No instruction-following or chat. The "task" is encoded in subtle format conventions (TL;DR, Q:/A:, bilingual pairs). The model has not learned to follow natural-language instructions per se. InstructGPT (2022) is what closes that gap.
  • No formal scaling law. The curves are empirical and suggestive but not quantified. Kaplan et al. (2020) does this; without that formalization, GPT-3's bet is hard to justify.
  • The strongest tasks are essentially LM tasks. CBT and LAMBADA and Winograd are all reducible to "score completions by LM likelihood." It's not clear how far this generalizes to tasks that aren't expressible as next-token prediction over natural-language candidates.
  • Performance distribution is far from training distribution. The model's failures concentrate exactly where the corpus is light: factual recall (no Wikipedia means weaker factoid QA), non-English content (mostly English corpus), and technical specialization (no domain corpora). Capabilities are downstream of corpus.
  • No discussion of compute or training cost. Training the 1.5B model took a non-trivial fraction of a TPU pod's time for weeks. The paper doesn't engage with the compute economics that will become central in later work.
  • No safety considerations. Famously, OpenAI initially withheld the 1.5B weights citing misuse concerns — but the paper itself contains essentially no discussion of the failure modes (toxic generation, hallucination, prompt injection) that would become the dominant concerns of the LLM era.

What's notably absent from the paper that would have been obvious in hindsight

  • "Emergence" framing. Later work (Wei et al. 2022) describes how some capabilities appear suddenly at a particular scale. GPT-2 doesn't frame its scaling curves that way — partly because at the scales tested, no qualitative emergence happens. Most improvements are smooth.
  • In-context learning as a named phenomenon. GPT-3 will identify "few-shot in-context learning" as a distinct capability. GPT-2 uses it (translation with bilingual pairs; QA with prior Q-A turns) but doesn't name it or center it.
  • The token-budget framing. Chinchilla (2022) will argue that for a given compute budget, GPT-2 (and GPT-3) were undertrained — too many parameters, not enough tokens. The paper doesn't engage with this trade-off because the concept doesn't yet exist.
Final mental model:

GPT-2 is the paper that showed language modeling is sufficient — at sufficient scale, on sufficient data, a vanilla LM does many of the things people had been building specialized supervised systems to do, simply because the supervised tasks are special cases of the natural distribution of text.

The architectural changes (pre-LN, $1/\sqrt{N}$ init, final LN, larger context, byte-level BPE) are stability and scope fixes that let the same architecture train at depth. The corpus changes (WebText, Reddit-karma filter, Wikipedia excluded) make the training distribution contain the task patterns the model will later be prompted with. The methodological choice (zero-shot only) isolates the LM's intrinsic capability from any test-task data fitting.

Out of those three deliberate moves comes the philosophical shift from pre-train + fine-tune to pre-train + prompt. Every modern LLM follows GPT-2's recipe, just bigger and on more data. The instructions and chat interfaces of 2022 onwards are bolted on top of the same skeleton.

GPT-1 invented the recipe. GPT-2 found out the recipe didn't need the fine-tuning step. The rest of the field has been re-running this experiment at larger and larger scale ever since.
