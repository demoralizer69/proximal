Part 1 — Abstract + Section 1 (Introduction)

The problem the paper is solving

Before this paper (2017), the dominant way to do sequence tasks — like translating English → French, or summarizing text — used Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), often the LSTM or GRU variants.

How RNNs work (intuition): They process a sequence one token at a time, carrying a "hidden state" forward — like reading a sentence word by word and updating your understanding after each word.

"The" → "cat" → "sat" → "on" → "the" → "mat" h₁ → h₂ → h₃ → h₄ → h₅ → h₆

Each hidden state $h_t$ depends on the previous one $h_{t-1}$. This is sequential by design.

Why this was a problem

  1. No parallelization within a sequence. You can't compute $h_5$ until $h_4$ is done. On GPUs (which thrive on parallelism), this is wasteful.
  2. Long-range dependencies are hard. If word 1 needs to influence word 100, the signal has to pass through 99 intermediate steps. Information gets diluted or lost.

The paper's pitch

"We propose a new simple network architecture, the Transformer, based solely on attention mechanisms, dispensing with recurrence and convolutions entirely."

Two big claims:

  1. Better quality — beats prior state-of-the-art on machine translation (WMT 2014 English-to-German and English-to-French).
  2. Much faster to train — because attention lets you process all positions in parallel.

The key idea in one sentence

Instead of passing information sequentially through hidden states, let every word directly look at every other word in the sequence and decide what's relevant. That "looking at" mechanism is called attention.

What "attention" means intuitively

Imagine translating: "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired."

To translate "it" correctly, the model needs to know "it" refers to "the animal" (not "the street"). Attention lets the model, when processing "it", directly attend to "animal" and pull information from it — no matter how far apart they are in the sentence.

Part 2 — Section 2 (Background)

This section answers: "Wasn't anyone else trying to fix RNNs? What were they doing?"

Prior approaches (Extended Neural GPU, ByteNet, ConvS2S — historical, don't memorize names) used convolutions instead of recurrence:

  • Convolutions can be parallelized ✅
  • BUT to connect two distant positions, you need many stacked conv layers — number of operations grows with distance: $O(n)$ or $O(\log n)$.

The Transformer's claim: connecting any two positions takes a constant number of operations — $O(1)$. Every position can look at every other position in a single attention step.

Trade-off: averaging information from many positions reduces effective resolution. The paper counters this with Multi-Head Attention (coming up).

Self-attention had been used before (in reading comprehension, summarization, etc.), but the Transformer is the first model to rely on self-attention alone — no RNNs, no convolutions.

Part 3 — Section 3.1 (Encoder–Decoder Stacks)

The Transformer follows the classic encoder-decoder structure used for sequence-to-sequence tasks like translation.

Input sentence → [ENCODER] → rich representation → [DECODER] → Output sentence (English) (German)

The Encoder

  • A stack of N = 6 identical layers.
  • Each layer has two sub-layers:
    1. Multi-head self-attention — each word looks at all words in the input.
    2. Position-wise feed-forward network — a small MLP applied to each position independently.

Around each sub-layer there are two important tricks:

(a) Residual connection: output = sublayer(x) + x — helps gradients flow during training.

(b) Layer normalization: normalizes activations to have stable statistics.

So each sub-layer actually computes: LayerNorm(x + Sublayer(x))

All sub-layers and embeddings produce vectors of dimension $d_{\text{model}} = 512$.

The Decoder

  • Also N = 6 identical layers.
  • Each layer has three sub-layers:
    1. Masked multi-head self-attention — each output position looks at previous output positions only.
    2. Multi-head attention over the encoder output — this is where the decoder "reads" the input sentence.
    3. Position-wise feed-forward network.

Why "masking" in the decoder?

When generating output (e.g., translating word by word), at position $i$ the model should only see positions $< i$. Otherwise, during training, it would "cheat" by peeking at the answer.

ENCODER (×6): ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ Multi-Head Self-Attention │ ← every word looks at every word │ ↓ + residual │ │ LayerNorm │ │ ↓ │ │ Feed-Forward Network │ │ ↓ + residual │ │ LayerNorm │ └─────────────────────────────┘ DECODER (×6): ┌──────────────────────────────────┐ │ Masked Multi-Head Self-Attention │ ← look only at past output words │ ↓ │ │ Multi-Head Attention (Enc→Dec) │ ← look at encoder output │ ↓ │ │ Feed-Forward Network │ └──────────────────────────────────┘

Doubts Answered: Residuals, LayerNorm, "Why +x?" just answered

"Motivation behind residual connection and layernorm? And why did we directly add x — why not log(x) or x²? Plus what is layernorm mathematically?"

1. Motivation for Residual Connections

The problem they solve: deep networks are hard to train.

(a) Vanishing gradients. During backpropagation, gradients are multiplied by the derivatives of each layer (chain rule). If those derivatives are small, gradients shrink exponentially as they travel backward. By the time they reach the early layers, they're too tiny to update the weights — early layers stop learning.

(b) The degradation problem. The original ResNet paper (He et al., 2015) showed something counterintuitive: deeper networks performed worse than shallower ones — even on training data (so it's not overfitting). Optimization just gets harder as depth grows.

The residual fix: instead of asking a layer to learn the full transformation $H(x)$, ask it to learn the residual $F(x) = H(x) - x$. Then:

$$H(x) = F(x) + x$$

Why this helps:

  • Gradient highway. In backprop, the gradient of $F(x) + x$ w.r.t. $x$ is $\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} + 1$. That +1 means gradients always have a clean path backward — even if $\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} \approx 0$, the gradient still flows.
  • Easy identity. If the optimal layer behavior is "pass through unchanged," the layer just needs to learn $F(x) \approx 0$ — easy. Without the skip, learning identity is surprisingly hard.
  • Reframes the task. The layer now learns "what to change about x," not "what x should become from scratch." Smaller, simpler target.

2. Why x and not log(x) or x²?

Great design question. Several reasons:

(a) Only identity gives derivative = 1. That clean gradient highway depends on the derivative of the skip term being constant 1.

  • $\frac{d}{dx}(x) = 1$ ✅ — gradient flows unchanged
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\log x) = \frac{1}{x}$ — explodes near 0, vanishes for large $x$; also undefined for $x \leq 0$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(x^2) = 2x$ — gradient scales with input magnitude, can explode or vanish

(b) Activations can be negative. Hidden states in neural nets are typically zero-centered. $\log(x)$ is undefined for negatives. $x^2$ destroys sign information (collapses $\pm x$ to the same value).

(c) Identity preserves the original signal exactly. A nonlinear skip would also be a transformation — you'd want to know "did this layer help?" relative to the unchanged input. The identity skip gives you a clean baseline.

(d) Alternatives have been tried. Highway Networks (Srivastava et al., 2015) used a gated skip: $T(x) \cdot F(x) + (1 - T(x)) \cdot x$, where $T$ is learned. ResNet's pure identity outperformed it — simpler, fewer parameters, faster, more stable.

(e) Optimization initialization. If weights of $F$ are initialized small, the block computes $\approx x$ at the start of training. The network starts from a sensible "do nothing" point and learns deviations from there. Any nonlinear skip would break this.

3. LayerNorm Mathematically

For a single input vector $\mathbf{x} = [x_1, x_2, \dots, x_d]$ (one position in the sequence, with $d$ features):

Step 1 — compute mean and variance across features:

$$\mu = \frac{1}{d} \sum_{i=1}^{d} x_i \quad\quad \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{d} \sum_{i=1}^{d} (x_i - \mu)^2$$

Step 2 — normalize:

$$\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}}$$

($\epsilon$ is a tiny constant like $10^{-5}$ to avoid divide-by-zero.)

Step 3 — scale and shift with learned parameters:

$$y_i = \gamma_i \cdot \hat{x}_i + \beta_i$$

where $\gamma$ and $\beta$ are learnable vectors of size $d$. They let the network "un-normalize" if it wants — giving it full expressive power.

Motivation for LayerNorm

  • Stable activation scale. Without normalization, activations can grow or shrink wildly through 6+ layers. LayerNorm pins them to mean 0, variance 1 → training is stable.
  • No batch dependence. Unlike BatchNorm (which normalizes across the batch), LayerNorm operates on a single sample. This matters for:
    • Variable-length sequences — batch statistics are awkward when sentences have different lengths.
    • Small batch sizes or batch size 1 at inference — BatchNorm misbehaves; LayerNorm is identical at train and test time.
  • Faster convergence. Empirically, LayerNorm makes training of deep stacks dramatically faster.

Key contrast: BatchNorm vs. LayerNorm

BatchNorm: normalize across BATCH for each feature → mean & var depend on other samples in the batch LayerNorm: normalize across FEATURES for each sample → mean & var depend only on this one sample

In Transformers, LayerNorm is the right choice because each token is processed somewhat independently, and we want consistency between training and inference.

Part 4 — Section 3.2.1: Scaled Dot-Product Attention NEW

⚠️ This is the most important concept in the entire paper. Take your time here — everything else builds on it.

The Q, K, V analogy 🔍

Think of attention like a search engine:

  • Q (Query): what you're searching for. "I'm the word 'it' — who do I refer to?"
  • K (Key): what each item advertises itself as. "I am the word 'animal', a noun, subject of the sentence."
  • V (Value): the actual content that gets returned. "Here's my meaning vector: [0.3, -0.7, 1.2, ...]"

How it works:

  1. The Query is compared against every Key → gives a similarity score.
  2. Scores are turned into weights (using softmax — they sum to 1).
  3. The output is a weighted sum of Values.

So: "Pull more content from items whose Keys match my Query."

The formula

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$

Let's unpack each piece, step by step.

Step 1: $QK^T$ — the similarity scores

  • $Q$ is a matrix where each row is a query vector (one per word).
  • $K$ is a matrix where each row is a key vector.
  • $QK^T$ gives you a matrix of dot products — how similar each query is to each key.

For one query vector $q$ and one key vector $k$:

  • $q \cdot k$ is large if they point in similar directions (high similarity).
  • $q \cdot k$ is near zero if they're unrelated.

If you have 6 words, $QK^T$ is a $6 \times 6$ matrix where entry $(i, j)$ = "how much should word $i$ attend to word $j$?"

Step 2: $\div \sqrt{d_k}$ — the scaling

$d_k$ is the dimension of the key vectors (in the paper: $d_k = 64$).

Why divide? When $d_k$ is large, dot products can become huge in magnitude. If the scores are huge, softmax produces extremely peaked distributions (one value $\approx 1$, the rest $\approx 0$), which kills gradients during training.

Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ keeps scores in a reasonable range. The $\sqrt{\cdot}$ comes from a statistical argument:

If $q$ and $k$ are vectors with independent components of mean 0 and variance 1, then $q \cdot k = \sum_{i=1}^{d_k} q_i k_i$ has mean 0 and variance $d_k$. Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ brings the variance back to 1.

Step 3: $\text{softmax}(\cdot)$ — turn scores into weights

Softmax converts the scores into a probability distribution along each row:

  • All weights are between 0 and 1.
  • Each row sums to 1.

Row $i$ now says: "Word $i$ should pay this much attention to each of the other words."

Mathematically, for a row of scores $s = [s_1, s_2, \dots, s_n]$:

$$\text{softmax}(s)_j = \frac{e^{s_j}}{\sum_{k=1}^{n} e^{s_k}}$$

Step 4: $\times V$ — weighted sum of values

Multiply by $V$ to actually retrieve a blend of value vectors, weighted by attention.

A concrete walk-through (toy example)

Sentence: "The cat sat" — 3 words.

Suppose for the word "sat":

  • Its query $q_{\text{sat}}$ is most similar to the key $k_{\text{cat}}$ (subject of the verb).
  • Less similar to $k_{\text{the}}$ (a generic article).

The scores (after $QK^T / \sqrt{d_k}$) might be:

"the":  0.5
"cat":  3.0   ← highest
"sat":  1.0

After softmax:

"the":  0.08
"cat":  0.78   ← gets most of the weight
"sat":  0.14

The output for "sat" is then:

$$\text{output}_{\text{sat}} = 0.08 \cdot v_{\text{the}} + 0.78 \cdot v_{\text{cat}} + 0.14 \cdot v_{\text{sat}}$$

So "sat" pulls in mostly the meaning of "cat" — it has learned that the verb depends on its subject. ✨

Where do Q, K, V come from? 🤔

This is the part that often confuses people.

For self-attention, all three come from the same input — your sequence of word embeddings $X$:

$$Q = XW^Q, \quad K = XW^K, \quad V = XW^V$$

$W^Q$, $W^K$, $W^V$ are learned weight matrices. They project the same input into three different "views":

  • Q-view: what each word is looking for.
  • K-view: what each word offers.
  • V-view: what each word contributes.

That's why it's called "self"-attention — the sequence attends to itself.

Doubts Answered: Variance Theory + Q/K/V Motivation just answered

"Does adding k independent variables with variance 1 each just make the variance k? Why does every step of normalization try to keep variance = 1? And what's the motivation behind the 3 matrices K, Q, V — how did they come up with this idea?"

1. Yes — variances add for independent variables

For independent random variables $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_k$:

$$\text{Var}(X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_k) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \text{Var}(X_i)$$

This comes from the general formula:

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\,\text{Cov}(X, Y)$$

When $X$ and $Y$ are independent, $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$, so the cross terms vanish. So if each $X_i$ has variance 1, the sum has variance $k$, and standard deviation $\sqrt{k}$.

Applying this to the attention dot product

The dot product $q \cdot k = \sum_{i=1}^{d_k} q_i k_i$. Let's check the variance of each term $q_i k_i$:

Assume $q_i, k_i$ are independent, each with mean 0 and variance 1.

Mean of $q_i k_i$:

$$E[q_i k_i] = E[q_i] \cdot E[k_i] = 0 \cdot 0 = 0$$

Variance of $q_i k_i$:

$$\text{Var}(q_i k_i) = E[(q_i k_i)^2] - (E[q_i k_i])^2 = E[q_i^2] \cdot E[k_i^2] - 0 = 1 \cdot 1 = 1$$

So each term has variance 1, and summing $d_k$ independent such terms gives variance $d_k$. Standard deviation = $\sqrt{d_k}$.

Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ rescales the standard deviation back to 1. ✅

2. Why obsessively keep variance ≈ 1?

This is the deeper question. The literal value "1" isn't magical — what matters is keeping variance CONSTANT through the layers. Here's the concrete reasoning:

(a) The exponential drift problem

If every layer multiplies variance by some factor $c$, after $L$ layers the variance is $c^L$:

  • $c > 1$ → variance explodes (e.g., $1.5^{20} \approx 3325$) → activations overflow
  • $c < 1$ → variance vanishes (e.g., $0.5^{20} \approx 10^{-6}$) → activations become indistinguishable

Either way, training breaks. The only stable choice is $c \approx 1$ — a fixed point.

(b) Activation functions have a "sweet spot"

Most nonlinearities (softmax, tanh, sigmoid) behave well when inputs are roughly in $[-2, 2]$:

  • Softmax with large inputs becomes a one-hot vector (one value $\approx 1$, rest $\approx 0$). Gradient through softmax is then essentially zero everywhere. This is exactly why $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling matters!
  • tanh/sigmoid with large inputs saturate at $\pm 1$ / 0 or 1 → derivative ≈ 0 → vanishing gradients.
  • tanh/sigmoid with tiny inputs are nearly linear → no nonlinearity → network can't learn complex functions.

(c) Variance = 1 specifically?

Why 1 and not 0.5 or 2? Three reasons:

  1. Standard normal convention. $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ is the canonical reference distribution. Initialization schemes (Xavier, He) are designed around it.
  2. Identity for multiplication. If variance is 1, repeatedly applying "variance-preserving" operations keeps it at 1 forever — clean fixed point.
  3. Empirically calibrated. Activation functions and softmax happen to behave well at this scale. Could you train with variance 2 throughout? Probably yes — but you'd be re-tuning everything (initializations, learning rate, optimizer betas). Variance 1 is a Schelling point that everyone has standardized on.

So when you see $\sqrt{d_k}$ in the attention formula, or LayerNorm, or $\frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}$ in Xavier init, they're all enforcing the same invariant: "keep variance at 1 throughout the network."

The whole field of "deep learning that actually trains" is largely the discipline of preventing variance drift.

3. Motivation for Q, K, V — Where did the idea come from?

This wasn't invented from scratch. It evolved through a lineage of papers, each adding one piece.

Lineage (rough timeline)

  1. seq2seq with attention (Bahdanau et al., 2014; Luong et al., 2015). The decoder of an RNN translation model needed to "look back" at the encoder. They computed alignment scores between decoder state and each encoder state, then output a weighted sum of encoder states.
    At this stage: just queries (decoder state) and one role for encoder states (both compared against AND retrieved from).
  2. Memory Networks / Neural Turing Machines (Weston et al., 2014; Graves et al., 2014). Explicit "memory" banks read by computing addressing weights from a query. Introduced the abstraction of "read from memory by similarity."
  3. Key-Value Memory Networks (Miller et al., 2016). Crucially: separated keys (used to compute attention weights) from values (returned as content). This is the moment the K/V split appears explicitly.
  4. The Transformer (2017) takes the Q/K/V abstraction, applies it to self-attention (the sequence attends to itself), and makes all three projections learned.

The core insight: decouple matching from retrieving

Imagine a library lookup:

  • Book title (Key): what gets matched against your query. Optimized for searchability.
  • Book contents (Value): what you actually take home. Optimized for usefulness.
  • Your search term (Query): what you're looking for.

Title and contents describe the same book, but they're optimized for different jobs. Forcing them to be the same vector would mean compromising one for the other.

Why not just use $X$ for all three?

Suppose you skip the projections and use $X$ directly: $\text{Attention}(X, X, X)$. Two problems:

(a) Symmetric attention. $X X^T$ is a symmetric matrix — meaning word A attends to word B with the same weight as B to A. But that's not how language works. A verb attends to its subject differently than the subject attends to its verb.

With three different projections, $Q K^T = (XW^Q)(XW^K)^T = X W^Q (W^K)^T X^T$. The combined matrix $W^Q (W^K)^T$ is generally not symmetric → asymmetric attention patterns are possible. ✅

(b) Three independent "knobs." Each projection gives the model an independent lever:

  • $W^Q$ controls "what should I look for?"
  • $W^K$ controls "how should I make myself findable?"
  • $W^V$ controls "what do I contribute when I'm attended to?"

A single shared matrix would collapse all three jobs into one — much less flexible.

(c) Dimensionality control. $W^Q, W^K$ project to dimension $d_k$ (in the paper: 64) which is smaller than $d_{\text{model}} = 512$. This is computational efficiency — you don't need full 512-dim vectors for matching, just enough capacity. $W^V$ projects to $d_v$ (also 64) for the value content.

The "soft database" mental model

The cleanest way to remember it: attention is a differentiable, soft database lookup.

Hard lookup (Python dict):    db = {key1: val1, key2: val2, ...}
                              result = db[query]   # exact match

Soft lookup (attention):      similarity = query · key_i  (for each i)
                              weights    = softmax(similarities)
                              result     = Σᵢ weights_i · val_i

It's a lookup that's differentiable (gradients flow through softmax and weighted sums), so the network can learn what to query, what to expose as a key, and what to return as a value — all from data.

Part 5 — Section 3.2.2: Multi-Head Attention NEW

The problem with a single attention head

Scaled dot-product attention (what we just learned) does one kind of mixing. For a given word, softmax produces one distribution over the other words — meaning that word ends up paying attention in one specific way.

But language has many simultaneous relationships:

  • Syntactic: "ran" depends on "she" (subject-verb agreement).
  • Semantic: "bank" relates to "river" (disambiguating word sense).
  • Coreference: "it" refers back to "animal".
  • Positional: the next word matters in a different way than the word 10 positions back.

A single attention head has to compromise — averaging all these signals into one weighted blend. The paper's fix: run multiple attention computations in parallel, then combine them.

The mechanism

Instead of one attention with full $d_{\text{model}}$-dimensional Q, K, V, the model uses $h$ parallel heads. Each head has its own learned projections.

$$\text{MultiHead}(Q, K, V) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \dots, \text{head}_h) \, W^O$$

where each head is:

$$\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(Q W^Q_i, \, K W^K_i, \, V W^V_i)$$

The shapes of the per-head matrices:

  • $W^Q_i, W^K_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_k}$
  • $W^V_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_v}$
  • $W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{h \cdot d_v \times d_{\text{model}}}$ (the final output projection)

In the paper:

  • $h = 8$ heads
  • $d_k = d_v = d_{\text{model}} / h = 512 / 8 = 64$

Why this works

(a) Each head can specialize

Because each head has its own $W^Q_i, W^K_i, W^V_i$, it learns to focus on a particular type of relationship. After training, you can often visualize attention patterns and see:

  • Head 1: attending to the previous word
  • Head 2: attending to the syntactic head of the phrase
  • Head 3: attending to similar tokens across the sentence
  • ... and so on.

It's like having 8 different experts looking at the sentence from different angles, then a final layer ($W^O$) deciding how to combine their reports.

(b) Computational cost is comparable to single-head

Crucial design point: total compute is roughly the same as one full-dimensional attention.

One big head ($d_k = 512$): $n \times n \times 512 = 512 n^2$ operations for the score matrix.

Eight small heads ($d_k = 64$ each): $8 \times n \times n \times 64 = 512 n^2$ operations.

Same total work, but partitioned into 8 independent "experts." This is parameter-efficient: you get diversity without paying extra compute.

(c) Better gradients through diversity

If one head's projection collapses (e.g., learns useless features), the others can still carry signal. Multi-head is more robust during training than putting all eggs in one head.

After concatenating the 8 head outputs you get a 10 × 512 matrix where the first 64 columns are head 1's output, next 64 are head 2's, etc. The columns are siloed by head.

$W^O$ is a learned $512 \times 512$ matrix that mixes information across heads — letting the model combine, weight, and recombine the per-head representations into a unified output. Without $W^O$, the heads would just be parallel slices that never interact.

The mental model

"Single-head attention is one expert looking at the sentence. Multi-head attention is a panel of experts (8 of them), each with a different specialty, whose reports get aggregated by a final mixer."

Doubt Answered: What Prevents Head Collapse? just answered

"What prevents the $W^Q$, $W^K$, etc. of the different heads from just learning to be the exact same?"

This is a fantastic question — and the honest answer is: nothing forces them to be different architecturally. No explicit constraint, no orthogonality penalty, no diversity loss. Heads diversify only through emergent training dynamics. Let's unpack what those dynamics are — and then look at the uncomfortable truth that this doesn't always work.

1. Random initialization breaks symmetry

Each head's projection matrices $W^Q_i$, $W^K_i$, $W^V_i$ are initialized with independent random draws (typically Xavier/Glorot or He init). So even before any training:

$$W^Q_1 \neq W^Q_2 \neq \dots \neq W^Q_8$$

This is the foundational reason. If all heads started with identical weights, they would receive identical gradients on every forward/backward pass, and remain identical forever — they'd be a single head in disguise. This is the classic "symmetry problem" in neural networks, and random initialization is the standard cure.

2. SGD amplifies tiny initial differences

Random initialization gives each head a slightly different starting position in the loss landscape. From there, gradient descent is sensitive to initial conditions:

  • Two heads with slightly different weights produce slightly different outputs.
  • Their slightly different outputs pull slightly different gradients.
  • The slightly different gradients widen the gap.
  • Over thousands of training steps, small differences compound into large ones.

This is the same dynamic that makes a butterfly flap matter — exponentially amplifying tiny initial differences along an unstable trajectory.

3. $W^O$ creates an implicit incentive for specialization

This is the deepest mechanism. Recall the final step:

$$\text{Output} = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \dots, \text{head}_8) \cdot W^O$$

$W^O$ is $512 \times 512$, and the concatenated input is split into 8 column-blocks of 64 features each, one per head. So $W^O$ can be thought of as 8 sub-matrices, each "reading from" one head:

columns 0..63 64..127 ... 448..511 ┌─────────┬─────────┬─────────┬─────────┐ │ block₁ │ block₂ │ ... │ block₈ │ ← these multiply head₁, head₂, ..., head₈ └─────────┴─────────┴─────────┴─────────┘ W^O

During backprop:

  • The gradient flowing into head $i$ depends on the rows of $W^O$ that consume head $i$'s columns.
  • If two heads produced identical outputs, the optimizer would have redundant capacity — half its degrees of freedom doing nothing. The loss can typically be reduced more by making them different.
  • So the gradient signal naturally pushes each head to contribute distinct information that the others aren't providing.

This is the same reason ensembles work better when models are diverse: a duplicate model contributes nothing new. Multi-head attention is essentially an "ensemble inside a layer" where the optimizer learns the ensemble weights ($W^O$) jointly with the experts.

4. Different downstream gradient paths

Even before $W^O$ is trained, each head sits at a different position in the concatenated output. So at the very first optimization step:

  • Head 1's output gets multiplied by the first 64 rows of $W^O$.
  • Head 2's output gets multiplied by rows 65–128.
  • ...

Since $W^O$ is also randomly initialized, each head sees a different downstream environment. The gradients propagated back to each head's $W^Q_i, W^K_i, W^V_i$ are therefore different from step one.

5. Reality check: heads do sometimes collapse

Despite all the above, here's the uncomfortable truth: multi-head attention often has redundant heads in practice. Research has shown this empirically.

Michel et al. (2019), "Are Sixteen Heads Really Better than One?" — they showed that you can prune most attention heads from a trained Transformer with minimal accuracy loss. In many layers, a single head was sufficient.

Other related findings:

  • Voita et al. (2019) found that only a small subset of heads carry specialized roles (positional, syntactic, rare-word); most are "generic" or redundant.
  • You can train Transformers with explicit diversity regularization (orthogonality penalties, etc.), but it's not standard — the default training works "well enough" most of the time.

So your intuition is correct: the architecture permits collapse, and partial collapse does happen. The reasons it isn't catastrophic:

  1. Even partial diversity is enough to give multi-head a measurable edge over single-head.
  2. The optimizer "wants" heads to be different because diverse heads reduce loss more — but the pressure is weak (just a soft incentive, not a hard constraint).
  3. Modern training has so much capacity and data that some wasted heads aren't a deal-breaker.

The summary mental model

Heads diversify through a chain of accidents: random init → different starting points → different gradients → different learning trajectories. The optimizer softly encourages diversity (because redundant heads waste capacity), but doesn't enforce it. In practice, you get partial diversity — some heads specialize beautifully, others end up redundant.

Side Concept: What Is an Ensemble? just answered

"What is an ensemble?"

The one-line definition

An ensemble is a collection of models whose individual predictions are combined (usually by averaging or voting) to produce a single, better prediction.

The simplest example: averaging

Say you want to predict tomorrow's temperature, and you have three models:

  • Model A predicts 22°C
  • Model B predicts 25°C
  • Model C predicts 24°C

A simple ensemble prediction: just average them → $(22 + 25 + 24) / 3 = 23.67$°C.

For classification (e.g., "is this image a cat or a dog?"), you'd do majority voting instead of averaging.

Why this works — the "wisdom of crowds" effect

Each model makes its own mistakes. If the mistakes are different (i.e., the models are diverse), they tend to cancel out when averaged.

Mathematically: suppose each model's prediction has some bias + some random error. If the errors are independent and zero-mean, averaging $N$ predictions reduces the error variance by a factor of $N$:

$$\text{Var}\!\left(\frac{1}{N} \sum_i \hat{y}_i\right) = \frac{1}{N^2} \sum_i \text{Var}(\hat{y}_i) = \frac{\sigma^2}{N}$$

(Same independence-variance argument we used for the dot product — variances add, then dividing by $N$ shrinks the average.)

Notice the critical requirement: the models must be diverse. If they all make the same mistakes, averaging doesn't help — you just get the same wrong answer.

Common ensemble techniques

Technique How it creates diversity
Bagging
(Random Forest is the classic example)		 Train each model on a different random subset of the training data. Different subsets → different models → different mistakes.
Boosting
(XGBoost, AdaBoost)		 Train models sequentially, where each new model focuses on the examples the previous models got wrong.
Stacking Train several "base" models, then train a "meta-model" that learns how to combine their predictions optimally.
Different random seeds Train multiple copies of the same model architecture with different random initializations. Each lands at a different local minimum.

Connecting back to multi-head attention

Multi-head attention is essentially an ensemble baked into a single layer:

  • Each "head" is like a base model — it computes an attention output independently.
  • $W^O$ is a learned stacking-style combiner — instead of simple averaging, it learns the optimal linear mixture.
  • Diversity comes from random init + SGD dynamics (like the "different random seeds" technique above).

The reason head collapse hurts is the same reason ensembles fail when models are too similar: you lose the variance-reduction effect. Eight identical heads = one head, not eight.

Real-world ML example you've likely encountered

If you've used Random Forest (e.g., in scikit-learn): it's an ensemble of decision trees. Each tree sees a different random sample of the data and a different random subset of features. No single tree is great, but the average over 100 trees is robust and accurate. Same principle — diversity + averaging beats any individual.

Part 6 — Section 3.2.3: Applications of Attention NEW

So far we've learned the mechanism of attention. Now: where is it actually used in the Transformer?

The paper uses multi-head attention in three distinct places, each with a different source for Q, K, V.

1. Encoder Self-Attention

Where: Inside each encoder layer (in all 6 layers).

Where do Q, K, V come from? All three come from the same place: the output of the previous encoder layer (or the input embeddings, in the first layer).

$$Q = X_{\text{enc}} W^Q, \quad K = X_{\text{enc}} W^K, \quad V = X_{\text{enc}} W^V$$

where $X_{\text{enc}}$ is the previous encoder layer's output.

What it does: Each position in the encoder attends to all other positions in the encoder. This lets every word in the input sentence see every other word — building up context-rich representations.

Example: When encoding "the bank by the river," the word "bank" can attend to "river" and pick up that this is a riverbank, not a financial institution.

2. Decoder Masked Self-Attention

Where: The first sub-layer inside each decoder layer (in all 6 layers).

Where do Q, K, V come from? Same as encoder self-attention — all from the previous decoder layer's output:

$$Q = X_{\text{dec}} W^Q, \quad K = X_{\text{dec}} W^K, \quad V = X_{\text{dec}} W^V$$

What's different? A mask is applied to prevent each position from attending to future positions.

Why masking is needed

The decoder generates output one token at a time (left-to-right). At position $i$, the model is trying to predict the next token. It must only see positions $0, 1, \dots, i$ — never $i+1, i+2, \dots$ Otherwise, during training, it would "cheat" by peeking at the answer it's supposed to predict.

This is called the auto-regressive property: each output depends only on previous outputs, never future ones.

How masking is implemented mechanically

Recall the attention score matrix: $QK^T / \sqrt{d_k}$, shape $n \times n$, row $i$ = scores for what position $i$ attends to.

The mask sets the scores for "future" positions (the upper triangular part of the matrix) to $-\infty$ (in practice, a very large negative number like $-10^9$) before the softmax:

For sequence length n = 5: Original scores (5×5): Masked scores (5×5): [s s s s s] [s −∞ −∞ −∞ −∞] [s s s s s] [s s −∞ −∞ −∞] [s s s s s] → mask → [s s s −∞ −∞] [s s s s s] [s s s s −∞] [s s s s s] [s s s s s ] ↑ Upper triangle replaced with −∞

After softmax, $e^{-\infty} = 0$, so those positions get weight = 0. The model literally cannot attend to them.

Why $-\infty$ before softmax instead of just zeroing out weights after? Because softmax weights have to sum to 1. If you zero them out post-softmax, the rest don't compensate. Adding $-\infty$ pre-softmax makes those entries vanish during normalization, and the remaining valid positions' weights re-distribute to still sum to 1. Clean and elegant.

3. Encoder-Decoder Attention (Cross-Attention)

Where: The second sub-layer inside each decoder layer.

Where do Q, K, V come from? This is the most interesting one — they come from different sources:

  • Queries (Q): from the previous sub-layer of the decoder (the masked self-attention output).
  • Keys (K) and Values (V): from the final output of the encoder.
$$Q = X_{\text{dec}} W^Q, \quad K = X_{\text{enc-out}} W^K, \quad V = X_{\text{enc-out}} W^V$$

What it does: This is where the decoder reads the input sentence. Each decoder position (a word being generated in the target language) attends over all positions of the encoded source sentence.

Example (English → German translation):

  • The decoder is generating the German word at position 3.
  • Its current state (after masked self-attention over German words so far) becomes the query.
  • The encoder's output (representations of the full English sentence) provides the keys and values.
  • The decoder asks: "Given what I've generated so far, which English words should I be paying attention to right now?"

This is essentially the same attention mechanism used in the original Bahdanau (2014) seq2seq model — the decoder peeking at the encoder. The Transformer just generalizes it with Q/K/V projections and multi-head structure.

Part 7 — Section 3.3: Position-wise Feed-Forward Networks NEW

Recall: each encoder layer has 2 sub-layers, each decoder layer has 3. We've covered the attention sub-layer in detail. The other sub-layer (present in both encoder and decoder) is the position-wise feed-forward network, or FFN.

The formula

$$\text{FFN}(x) = \max(0, \, xW_1 + b_1)\, W_2 + b_2$$

It's a 2-layer fully-connected network (an MLP) with a ReLU nonlinearity in between:

  1. Linear layer 1: $xW_1 + b_1$ — projects from $d_{\text{model}} = 512$ up to $d_{\text{ff}} = 2048$.
  2. ReLU: $\max(0, \cdot)$ — applied element-wise. Sets negatives to 0, keeps positives.
  3. Linear layer 2: $W_2 + b_2$ — projects back down from $d_{\text{ff}} = 2048$ to $d_{\text{model}} = 512$.

Shapes:

  • $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{512 \times 2048}$
  • $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{2048 \times 512}$
  • $b_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{2048}, \, b_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{512}$

Visual

input x (512-dim per token) │ ▼ ┌──────────┐ │ Linear 1 │ 512 → 2048 (W₁, b₁) └────┬─────┘ │ ▼ ┌──────────┐ │ ReLU │ max(0, ·) └────┬─────┘ │ ▼ (2048-dim "expanded" representation) ┌──────────┐ │ Linear 2 │ 2048 → 512 (W₂, b₂) └────┬─────┘ │ ▼ output (512-dim per token)

What does "position-wise" mean?

This is the key word. The same FFN — same weights $W_1, W_2, b_1, b_2$ — is applied independently at every position in the sequence.

Important: the FFN has no cross-position interaction. Word 5 doesn't see word 2 inside the FFN. The mixing across positions happened in the attention sub-layer; the FFN just refines each position individually.

Sequence of n tokens (each 512-dim) entering the FFN: pos 1 ──► FFN ──► pos 1 output pos 2 ──► FFN ──► pos 2 output ← same FFN weights pos 3 ──► FFN ──► pos 3 output applied independently pos 4 ──► FFN ──► pos 4 output at every position ... pos n ──► FFN ──► pos n output NO information flows between positions inside the FFN.

Why expand to 2048 and back down to 512?

The inner dimension $d_{\text{ff}} = 2048$ is 4× larger than $d_{\text{model}} = 512$. This "expand-then-contract" pattern is common in deep networks (also called a "bottleneck inverted" or "inverted bottleneck").

Why bother?

  • Capacity in the middle. The expanded 2048-dim hidden layer gives the network room to compute complex nonlinear features. With ReLU, each of the 2048 neurons can act as a learned feature detector.
  • Project back to fit the architecture. The next layer expects 512-dim input (because of residual connections — $\text{output} + x$ requires matching shapes). So we must project back.
  • Most of the model's parameters live here. $W_1$ has $512 \times 2048 \approx 1M$ parameters; $W_2$ has another 1M. The FFN dominates the parameter count over attention. This is where the model stores most of its "knowledge."

Why is FFN needed at all? Isn't attention enough?

Crucial point: attention is essentially linear in $V$. The output of $\text{softmax}(QK^T) V$ is a weighted linear combination of value vectors. The only nonlinearity is the softmax — and that operates only on the attention weights, not on the values themselves.

If you stacked attention layers without any feed-forward in between, the whole stack would be (almost) a giant linear function of the input — limited expressive power.

The FFN's job is to inject per-position nonlinearity (via ReLU). Together:

  • Attention mixes information across positions.
  • FFN transforms the mixed result nonlinearly at each position.

Alternating these two operations is what lets the Transformer learn rich, deep representations.

Alternative view: $1 \times 1$ convolutions

The paper notes you can also think of the FFN as two convolutions with kernel size 1:

  • A 1×1 conv with 2048 filters on a 512-channel input = $W_1$.
  • Followed by ReLU.
  • A 1×1 conv with 512 filters on the 2048-channel result = $W_2$.

This is mathematically identical to applying the same linear layers at every position. The "conv" framing is just a different vocabulary borrowed from vision.

Parameter sharing rules

  • Within one layer: the same $W_1, W_2$ are reused across every position of the sequence.
  • Across different layers: each of the 6 encoder layers and 6 decoder layers has its own $W_1, W_2$ — they are not shared between layers.

So in total, there are 12 different FFNs in the model (6 encoder + 6 decoder), each with ~2M parameters.

Mental model

Each Transformer block alternates: "talk to your neighbors" (attention) and "think hard on your own" (FFN). The FFN is where each token, having gathered context from attention, transforms that into something more useful for the next layer.

Doubt Answered: Why Non-linearity, and Why ReLU? just answered

"Why is non-linearity necessary — what value does it add? What's the intuition behind ReLU or sigmoid, and why is ReLU used here?"

1. Why is non-linearity necessary?

The mathematical reason: stacking linear layers collapses

Suppose you stack two linear layers with no nonlinearity in between:

$$y_1 = W_1 x + b_1$$
$$y_2 = W_2 y_1 + b_2 = W_2 (W_1 x + b_1) + b_2 = \underbrace{(W_2 W_1)}_{\text{just one matrix}} x + \underbrace{(W_2 b_1 + b_2)}_{\text{just one bias}}$$

That's the same as a single linear layer with effective weight $W_2 W_1$ and bias $W_2 b_1 + b_2$. No matter how many linear layers you stack — 6, 60, 600 — the entire stack collapses to one linear function.

So without non-linearity, deep networks are no more expressive than shallow ones. The whole point of "depth" disappears.

What linear models can and can't do

A linear function can only carve up input space with flat decision boundaries (hyperplanes). The canonical example is XOR:

XOR problem: │ 1 ●─┼─○ 0 │ ────┼──── │ 0 ○─┼─● 1 │ Two classes (● and ○). NO single straight line can separate them. A linear classifier fails here. A 2-layer network with non-linearity solves it trivially.

More generally, the Universal Approximation Theorem (Cybenko 1989, Hornik 1991) says: a feed-forward network with even a single hidden layer and a non-linear activation can approximate any continuous function on a compact domain, given enough hidden units.

Without non-linearity, you can only approximate linear functions. With it, you can approximate any function. Non-linearity is what gives neural networks their expressive power.

Intuition: bending the space

Imagine each linear layer as a rigid transformation of space (rotation, scaling, shearing) — but it can't bend. A non-linear activation acts like a fold: it warps the space, making things that were tangled into things that are separable.

Each layer of a deep network: (rigid transform) → (bend) → (rigid transform) → (bend) → ... The composition can sculpt very complex shapes.

2. Intuition behind common activations

Sigmoid: $\sigma(x) = \dfrac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}$

  • Range: $(0, 1)$.
  • Shape: smooth S-curve. At $x \to -\infty$, $\sigma \to 0$. At $x \to +\infty$, $\sigma \to 1$. At $x = 0$, $\sigma = 0.5$.
  • Intuition: a "soft on/off switch." Small inputs → close to 0 (off). Large inputs → close to 1 (on). Intermediate inputs → smoothly between. Often used to model probabilities.
σ(x) = 1 / (1 + e^(-x)) 1 ┤ ╱───────── │ ╱ 0.5 ┤ ● │ ╱ 0 ┤────── ────┼──── 0

Sigmoid's problem: vanishing gradients

The derivative is $\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1 - \sigma(x))$. Maximum value at $x=0$: $0.5 \cdot 0.5 = 0.25$. For $|x| > 4$ or so, the derivative is essentially zero (the curve flattens out — "saturates").

In a deep network, gradients flow back via the chain rule, multiplying together derivatives at each layer. If each layer's max derivative is 0.25, after 10 layers the gradient is at most $0.25^{10} \approx 10^{-6}$ — effectively zero. Early layers stop learning. This is why sigmoid is rarely used in deep hidden layers anymore.

ReLU: $f(x) = \max(0, x)$

  • Range: $[0, \infty)$.
  • Shape: Flat 0 for $x < 0$. Identity (line $y = x$) for $x \geq 0$. A simple "kink" at the origin.
  • Intuition: a "feature detector." Output is the input if the neuron's input is positive (the neuron "fires"), otherwise 0 (it's silent). Mimics, roughly, the firing behavior of biological neurons.
ReLU(x) = max(0, x) │ ╱ │ ╱ │ ╱ 0 ┤───────●───── ────────┼──── 0 "Half-wave rectification" from electronics — same idea.

Why ReLU dominates sigmoid in modern deep networks

  1. No vanishing gradient on the positive side. The derivative is 1 for any $x > 0$. Gradients flow back unchanged through positively-activated neurons. (Compare: sigmoid's max derivative is 0.25.)
  2. Computationally trivial. Just a comparison + max. No exponentials. Fast on hardware.
  3. Sparsity. Roughly half the neurons end up at 0 for any given input. This is computationally efficient and acts as implicit regularization (only relevant features "fire").
  4. Empirically faster convergence. Networks with ReLU train several times faster than sigmoid-based equivalents (shown in Krizhevsky et al., 2012, the AlexNet paper).

ReLU's downsides

  • Dead neurons. If a neuron's input becomes very negative and stays there, it outputs 0 forever — and since the derivative for $x < 0$ is also 0, no gradient flows back to update it. It's "dead." Common workaround: Leaky ReLU ($\max(0.01 x, x)$), which gives a small negative slope so dead neurons can recover.
  • Not zero-centered. Outputs are non-negative, which can introduce some bias in gradients. Usually a minor issue.
  • Not smooth at zero. The "kink" is mathematically a non-differentiable point. In practice, frameworks just define the derivative as 0 there and it's fine.

Quick comparison table

Activation Range Max gradient Compute Pros / Cons
Sigmoid (0, 1) 0.25 expensive (exp) Probability-like output; vanishing gradients in deep nets
tanh (−1, 1) 1.0 expensive (exp) Zero-centered (better than sigmoid); still saturates
ReLU [0, ∞) 1 (positive side) trivial Fast, no vanishing on positive side; can have dead neurons
GELU (newer) ≈ (−0.17, ∞) ~1.1 expensive Smooth ReLU-like; used in BERT, GPT

3. Why ReLU specifically in the Transformer's FFN?

  1. It was the default in 2017. When this paper was written, ReLU was the standard activation for fully-connected and conv layers — best known to work well, simple, fast.
  2. Avoids vanishing gradient in deep stacks. The Transformer is 6 layers deep on each side (encoder + decoder), each with an FFN. Sigmoid would have caused trouble.
  3. Computationally cheap. The FFN already has ~2M parameters per layer; you want activations to be fast.
  4. Plays well with residual connections. Identity skip + ReLU together is well-tested at this point (ResNet, 2015).

What modern Transformers use

Most large language models after ~2018 replaced ReLU with GELU (Gaussian Error Linear Unit) — used in BERT, GPT-2/3/4, etc. GELU is roughly ReLU but smoother near zero. Empirically gives small but consistent improvements. Some recent models use SwiGLU or Swish.

But for understanding the original paper: ReLU is the right answer.

Mental model

Linear layers do "stretching, rotating, shearing." Non-linearities do "folding." A deep network is alternating stretches and folds. Without folds (no non-linearity), no matter how many stretches, you can't make complex shapes. ReLU is the simplest, fastest fold: everything below zero gets squashed to zero; everything above stays as is.

Part 8 — Section 3.4: Embeddings and Softmax NEW

This section is short. It addresses two things at the boundaries of the model:

  • Input side: How do raw tokens (words/subwords) become the 512-dim vectors that go into the encoder/decoder?
  • Output side: How does the decoder's final output get converted into actual token predictions?

1. Input embeddings

The model's vocabulary contains some fixed number of tokens — say $V = 37{,}000$ subword units (the paper uses byte-pair encoding for English-German).

Each token gets a learned vector of dimension $d_{\text{model}} = 512$. This is just a giant lookup table:

$$E \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times d_{\text{model}}}$$

Row $i$ of $E$ is the embedding for token $i$. To embed a token, look up its row. To embed a sequence of $n$ tokens, look up $n$ rows → get an $n \times d_{\text{model}}$ matrix. This is the "X" we've been using everywhere.

Both the encoder (for source language tokens) and the decoder (for target language tokens) use embedding layers like this.

2. Output transformation: linear + softmax

The decoder produces an $n \times 512$ output. To predict the next token, you need a distribution over the vocabulary (37,000 numbers summing to 1).

Two steps:

  1. Linear: multiply by a $512 \times V$ matrix → gives $n \times V$ logits (one logit per vocab token per position).
  2. Softmax: apply softmax across the vocabulary dimension → gives $n \times V$ probabilities.
$$\text{logits} = X_{\text{dec-out}} \cdot W_{\text{out}}, \quad \text{probs} = \text{softmax}(\text{logits})$$

At position $i$, the model outputs a probability distribution over the next token; you take argmax (greedy decoding) or sample to pick the next word.

3. Weight tying (the clever bit)

Here's the design choice from this section:

"We share the same weight matrix between the two embedding layers and the pre-softmax linear transformation."

That means: the matrix $E$ (used to embed input tokens) is the same matrix as $W_{\text{out}}$ (used to map decoder output → vocab logits), just transposed:

$$W_{\text{out}} = E^T$$

Why share these weights?

(a) Conceptual symmetry. Embedding maps token → vector. The pre-softmax linear maps vector → token logits. They're inverse-flavored operations. Sharing the matrix says: "the same notion of token-to-vector should govern both directions."

(b) Parameter savings. The embedding matrix is huge: $V \times d_{\text{model}} = 37{,}000 \times 512 \approx 19M$ parameters. Sharing saves $19M$ params each on the input embedding and the output projection. Three places × 19M = ~57M params, reduced to one shared 19M.

(c) Regularization. Forcing the same matrix to do both jobs is a constraint that often improves generalization (less overfitting, smoother training).

This trick comes from Press & Wolf (2017), "Using the Output Embedding to Improve Language Models."

4. The mysterious $\sqrt{d_{\text{model}}}$ scaling

One detail in the paper:

"In the embedding layers, we multiply those weights by $\sqrt{d_{\text{model}}}$."

So the actual input to the model is:

$$X = E[\text{tokens}] \cdot \sqrt{d_{\text{model}}}$$

Why scale up by $\sqrt{512} \approx 22.6$?

Because of how embeddings are initialized: typical init gives each entry of $E$ small variance (e.g., $\sim 1/d_{\text{model}}$). So an embedding vector has norm $\sim 1$.

But the positional encoding (next section!) that gets added to the embedding has fixed magnitude — each component is in $[-1, 1]$ and the vector has norm $\sim \sqrt{d_{\text{model}}/2}$.

If you didn't scale up the embedding, the positional encoding would dominate in magnitude — the model would see "position" much more loudly than "token." Multiplying the embedding by $\sqrt{d_{\text{model}}}$ rescales it to be on the same order of magnitude as the positional encoding.

Without scaling: With ×√d_model scaling: embedding: [norm ≈ 1] embedding × √512: [norm ≈ √512] positional: [norm ≈ 16] positional: [norm ≈ 16] ───────────────────────── ───────────────────────────── positional drowns token both contribute comparably identity.

It's a clean, easy fix to a real numerical imbalance.

Mental model

Embedding = "every token in the vocab has an address (a 512-dim vector); the model carries the address around as the token's representation."

Pre-softmax linear = the inverse: "given a 512-dim vector, how well does it match each vocab token's address?" Sharing the matrix says the addresses are the same in both directions.

Clarification: What is "n" in the decoder output? just answered

"What is 'n' in the decoder output — is it the number of input tokens or output tokens? Please explain in more detail."

Quick answer

In $X_{\text{dec-out}}$ shape $n \times d_{\text{model}}$: $n$ is the number of output (target) tokens the decoder has processed so far, NOT the input length.

Critically, the meaning of "so far" differs between training and inference — which is where most confusion comes from. Let's go through both.

Concrete example

Let's translate "the cat sat" (English) → "die Katze saß" (German). Three source tokens, three target tokens. We'll use:

  • $n_{\text{src}} = 3$ for source length (English)
  • $n_{\text{tgt}} = 3$ for target length (German), or $4$ if we count the start-of-sentence token

What happens during TRAINING

During training, the entire target sentence is known. We process it in parallel using a trick called teacher forcing.

Step 1: Encoder processes the source

English tokens: [the, cat, sat] shape: (3,) After embedding: X_enc shape: (3, 512) After 6 encoder layers: enc_output shape: (3, 512) └─ n_src = 3

Step 2: Decoder processes a SHIFTED version of the target

The decoder gets the target sentence shifted right by one, with a special <start> token prepended:

Target (shifted): [<start>, die, Katze] shape: (3,) └─ n_tgt = 3 After embedding: X_dec shape: (3, 512) After 6 decoder layers: dec_output shape: (3, 512) └─ n_tgt = 3

The decoder output has shape $3 \times 512$. So $n = 3$ here is the number of target tokens (with the start token in place of the last position).

Step 3: Project to vocab and softmax

dec_output × W_out → logits softmax → probs (3 × 512) (512 × V) (3 × V) (3 × V) Where V = vocabulary size (~37,000)

Now we have 3 probability distributions over the vocabulary, one per position:

Decoder input at position $i$ Decoder output predicts: Compared against (true label):
$i=0$: <start> probability over next token "die"
$i=1$: die probability over next token "Katze"
$i=2$: Katze probability over next token "saß"

The decoder at position $i$ tries to predict the token at position $i+1$. We sum up the cross-entropy losses across all 3 positions and backprop. All 3 predictions happen in parallel (one forward pass), thanks to the causal mask preventing position $i$ from seeing positions $> i$.

This is "teacher forcing": even though position 2's input is the true "die" (not the model's guess), the model still tries to predict "Katze" from it.

What happens during INFERENCE (generating a translation)

At inference, the target is unknown — that's what we're trying to produce. So we generate one token at a time, growing $n$ by 1 each step.

Step 1: Encoder runs once

Same as training. We compute enc_output with shape $(n_{\text{src}}, 512) = (3, 512)$. This is reused for every decoder step.

Step 2: Decoder generates one token at a time

Step 1: Decoder input: [<start>] n = 1 dec_output shape: (1, 512) project + softmax → (1, V) argmax of last position → "die" Append "die" to output. Step 2: Decoder input: [<start>, die] n = 2 dec_output shape: (2, 512) project + softmax → (2, V) look at row 1 (the LAST position) → predict "Katze" Append "Katze". Step 3: Decoder input: [<start>, die, Katze] n = 3 dec_output shape: (3, 512) project + softmax → (3, V) look at row 2 (the last position) → predict "saß" Append "saß". Step 4: Decoder input: [<start>, die, Katze, saß] n = 4 predict next → "<end>" Stop.

So at inference, $n$ is the number of target tokens generated so far — it grows by 1 each step. Only the prediction at the last position is used; the earlier positions are recomputed but their predictions get discarded.

Why bother computing all $n$ positions in training?

Because all $n$ predictions are useful as training signal — each position predicts the next token, and we have ground truth for all of them. Training in parallel gives us $n_{\text{tgt}}$ supervision signals per sentence in a single forward pass. That's a huge efficiency win over RNNs.

The two "$n$"s in the architecture

So the lesson is: there are two different sequence lengths in the model.

Quantity Length Meaning
Encoder input / output $n_{\text{src}}$ Source sentence length
Decoder input / output $n_{\text{tgt}}$ Target tokens so far (full target during training; growing during inference)

These two lengths can be different. The encoder self-attention is over $n_{\text{src}} \times n_{\text{src}}$. The decoder self-attention is over $n_{\text{tgt}} \times n_{\text{tgt}}$. The cross-attention is over $n_{\text{tgt}} \times n_{\text{src}}$ (queries from decoder, keys/values from encoder) — the decoder's $n_{\text{tgt}}$ queries each attend to the encoder's $n_{\text{src}}$ keys/values.

Going back to Section 3.4

When I wrote "the decoder produces an $n \times 512$ output," I should have been clearer: $n = n_{\text{tgt}}$, the target sequence length. The pre-softmax linear projects each of those $n_{\text{tgt}}$ rows into a vocab-sized logit vector, one prediction per position.

Part 9 — Section 3.5: Positional Encoding NEW

The motivation: attention is "order-blind"

Here's a striking property of self-attention: if you shuffle the input tokens, attention computes the same outputs, just in shuffled order.

Why? Because attention computes:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$

None of these operations care about the row order of $Q$, $K$, $V$. The dot products, softmax, and weighted sum are all "permutation equivariant" — shuffle the inputs, the outputs shuffle the same way, but no information about position ever enters the computation.

This is a problem. Language has order:

  • "dog bites man" ≠ "man bites dog"
  • "not happy" ≠ "happy not"

Contrast this with RNNs (which process tokens sequentially → position is implicit) and CNNs (which use kernels over neighboring positions → position is implicit). The Transformer has no recurrence and no convolution, so position must be injected explicitly.

The solution: add a "position vector" to each token's embedding

For every position $\text{pos} \in \{0, 1, 2, \dots, n-1\}$, we generate a $d_{\text{model}}$-dimensional vector $\text{PE}(\text{pos})$. We add it to the token embedding:

$$\text{input}_{\text{pos}} = \text{Embedding}(\text{token}_{\text{pos}}) \cdot \sqrt{d_{\text{model}}} \;+\; \text{PE}(\text{pos})$$

Same token at different positions → different inputs. The model can now distinguish them.

The formula

The paper defines $\text{PE}$ using sines and cosines of varying frequencies:

$$\text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i) = \sin\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)$$
$$\text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i+1) = \cos\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)$$

Where:

  • $\text{pos}$ = position in the sequence (0, 1, 2, ...)
  • $i$ ranges from 0 to $d_{\text{model}}/2 - 1$ — indexing pairs of dimensions
  • Even dimensions get the sine; odd dimensions get the cosine.

So position 0 gets the vector $\text{PE}(0)$, position 1 gets $\text{PE}(1)$, etc. — each is a 512-dim vector built from many sine/cosine values at different frequencies.

Concrete view: what does $\text{PE}$ look like?

For $d_{\text{model}} = 4$ (toy example), the encoding for position $\text{pos}$ is:

$$\text{PE}(\text{pos}) = \left[\sin\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{0}}\right),\; \cos\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{0}}\right),\; \sin\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2/4}}\right),\; \cos\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2/4}}\right)\right]$$

Simplified:

$$\text{PE}(\text{pos}) = \left[\sin(\text{pos}),\; \cos(\text{pos}),\; \sin(\text{pos}/100),\; \cos(\text{pos}/100)\right]$$

Notice: the first pair oscillates fast (with $\text{pos}$ directly), the second pair oscillates slowly (with $\text{pos}/100$). Each pair of dimensions is like a different clock running at a different speed.

Intuition: think of it like a binary clock with smooth dials

Imagine you're trying to give each position a unique "address." A simple way: binary numbers.

pos = 0: 0 0 0 pos = 1: 0 0 1 pos = 2: 0 1 0 pos = 3: 0 1 1 pos = 4: 1 0 0 ... bit 0 oscillates fastest (period 2) bit 1 next (period 4) bit 2 slowest (period 8)

Each bit is a different frequency oscillator. Combine them and you get a unique pattern per number.

Positional encoding is the continuous (smooth) analog of this. Instead of binary bits, you have sine waves at different wavelengths. The wavelengths range geometrically from $2\pi$ (fastest) to $10000 \cdot 2\pi$ (slowest). With 256 such oscillators (256 pairs in 512 dimensions), each position gets a unique, smooth fingerprint.

Why sines and cosines specifically? The clever property

This is the deepest reason. With sinusoidal encoding, the position $k$ steps away is a fixed linear transformation of the current position.

Specifically:

$$\sin(a + b) = \sin(a)\cos(b) + \cos(a)\sin(b)$$
$$\cos(a + b) = \cos(a)\cos(b) - \sin(a)\sin(b)$$

So for any fixed offset $k$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \text{PE}(\text{pos}+k, 2i) \\ \text{PE}(\text{pos}+k, 2i+1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\omega_i k) & \sin(\omega_i k) \\ -\sin(\omega_i k) & \cos(\omega_i k) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i) \\ \text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i+1) \end{bmatrix}$$

where $\omega_i = 1 / 10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}$. This is a rotation matrix — the position-$(pos+k)$ encoding is just the position-$pos$ encoding rotated by an angle depending only on $k$.

What does that mean? The model can easily learn relative positional relationships through linear weights in its attention layers. "Attend to the token 3 positions ago" is achievable because the offset-3 transformation is the same linear map everywhere. The model just needs to learn that one linear map.

Bonus property: can extrapolate beyond training lengths

Since the formula is well-defined for any position (even ones the model has never seen during training), the model can — in principle — handle sequences longer than the longest training example. Just keep computing sines and cosines at larger $\text{pos}$ values.

(In practice, the model's performance still degrades on much-longer-than-trained inputs, but the encoding itself doesn't break. This was a stated motivation for choosing sinusoids over learned position embeddings.)

Why ADD, not CONCATENATE?

The PE is added to the token embedding, both 512-dim, producing a 512-dim result.

Why add?

  • Keeps the dimensionality constant. Concatenation would produce a $1024$-dim vector, requiring everything downstream to be wider.
  • No extra parameters. Addition is free.
  • The model can "separate" the two contributions implicitly. The token embedding is learned freely; the positional encoding has a specific spectral shape (sines/cosines at fixed frequencies). The model's linear layers can — through their learned weights — pay attention to certain frequency components in the input, effectively isolating positional vs. semantic info.

Sinusoidal vs. learned positional embeddings

The paper notes they also tried learned positional embeddings (just a $(\text{max\_len} \times d_{\text{model}})$ matrix of free parameters, indexed by position):

"We chose the sinusoidal version because it may allow the model to extrapolate to sequence lengths longer than the ones encountered during training."

The results were comparable. Modern transformers (BERT, GPT-2/3) typically use learned positional embeddings. More recent models (PaLM, LLaMA) use variants like Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) which apply the rotation idea more elegantly inside the attention computation itself.

Each token, after embedding, is moved into a specific "neighborhood" of 512-dim space based on its position. Tokens close in position get nearby vectors added; tokens far apart get distant vectors. The model can then learn to "navigate" by these positional landmarks — recognizing that "the token 5 steps back" always involves a specific rotation/offset.

6. Information flow summary

  • Encoder: input tokens flow upward through 6 layers, getting progressively richer representations through self-attention (gathering context) and FFN (refining each position). Output: $n_{\text{src}} \times 512$ — a rich representation of the source sentence.
  • Decoder: target tokens flow upward through 6 layers. Each layer does (1) masked self-attention over what's been generated so far, (2) cross-attention reading from the encoder output, (3) FFN. The encoder's output is consulted by every decoder layer (not just once).

Doubts: Why $W_{\text{out}} = E^T$? Why Don't We Subtract PE? just answered

"Why is $W_{\text{out}} = E^T$? Why don't we 'subtract' the positional encoding from the output of the decoder?"

Question 1: Why $W_{\text{out}} = E^T$ — what's the geometric reason?

Let's look at what the output projection is actually computing. The decoder produces a hidden state $h \in \mathbb{R}^{512}$ at each position. We compute:

$$\text{logits} = h \cdot W_{\text{out}}$$

If $W_{\text{out}} = E^T$, then for each token $v$ in the vocabulary, the logit is:

$$\text{logit}_v = h \cdot e_v$$

where $e_v$ is row $v$ of $E$ — the embedding vector of token $v$.

The geometric meaning

The embedding matrix $E$ assigns every token a fixed location in 512-dim space:

  • $e_{\text{cat}}$ = "where 'cat' lives"
  • $e_{\text{dog}}$ = "where 'dog' lives"
  • $e_{\text{run}}$ = "where 'run' lives"

The decoder's hidden state $h$ represents "the direction the next token should be in." Then:

$$\text{logit}_v = h \cdot e_v = |h| \cdot |e_v| \cdot \cos(\theta_{h, e_v})$$

The dot product is large when $h$ and $e_v$ point in similar directions. So the logit for token $v$ is high precisely when "the prediction direction $h$ is aligned with token $v$'s location."

512-dim semantic space: e_cat ● / h / e_dog ● ←(aligned!) ● ● e_run e_table h aligns with e_cat → high logit for "cat" h far from e_table → low logit for "table"

Why this makes deep conceptual sense

If we used a separate $W_{\text{out}}$ (not $E^T$), the model would need to learn two parallel semantic spaces:

  • One for representing tokens as input (the embedding $E$).
  • One for matching tokens as output ($W_{\text{out}}$).

That's wasteful and incoherent. Why should "the location of 'cat' in input-space" be different from "the location of 'cat' in prediction-space"? It's the same word.

Tying $W_{\text{out}} = E^T$ enforces: one semantic space, used in both directions.

Three concrete benefits

  1. Conceptual elegance. Input embedding and output matching share the same word geometry.
  2. ~38M fewer parameters. $V \times d_{\text{model}}$ = ~19M params; would be needed twice (input + output) without tying.
  3. Acts as regularization. Forcing both directions to share the same matrix is a strong constraint that improves generalization. Press & Wolf (2017) showed empirically this improves perplexity.

Question 2: Why don't we subtract positional encoding from the decoder output?

This is a sharp question. Your intuition is logical:

"We added $\text{PE}(\text{pos})$ at the input. Six layers later, the decoder output still 'contains' position info. The output projection $E^T$ is purely about semantic content (which token is what). Shouldn't we subtract PE first to get a 'clean' semantic representation?"

The answer is no — and unpacking why teaches you something important about how deep networks process information.

Reason 1: After 6 layers, there's no "PE part" to subtract

PE was added at the very bottom: $X_0 = E[\text{tokens}] \cdot \sqrt{d_{\text{model}}} + \text{PE}$.

But then $X_0$ went through 6 layers of:

  • Self-attention (a nonlinear, content-dependent mixing across positions)
  • Residual + LayerNorm
  • FFN (two linear layers with ReLU in between)
  • Residual + LayerNorm

By the time we reach $X_6$ (the decoder output), the PE is not a clean additive term anymore. It's been thoroughly transformed and entangled with token content. There is no "PE component" lurking inside $X_6$ that you could surgically extract.

Subtracting $\text{PE}(\text{pos})$ from $X_6$ would be like trying to "un-blend" a smoothie by pouring banana extract on it. It doesn't undo anything.

Reason 2: PE's job is done — it's consumed, not persisted

The purpose of PE is to break attention's order-blindness. PE feeds attention layers position information so they can compute "who's near whom." Once attention has used that info to compute its weights, the PE has done its job. It's not meant to ride along all the way to the output.

Think of PE as a signal for early layers. It doesn't need to be preserved or removed at the end — its useful work happened at the beginning, when attention was figuring out positional relationships.

Reason 3: The network learns to handle this — that's its job

The output projection ($E^T$) is learnable. Through training, $E$ adapts so that "decoder output at position $i$" produces good logits, whatever shape it happens to take.

The model doesn't need a clean "semantic-only" representation to project from. It just needs some representation that, when dot-producted with $E$, gives the right answer. End-to-end gradient descent figures out the rest.

If lingering position information helps the model (e.g., predicting <end> token at position $n$), it'll use it. If it hurts, training will push the decoder to suppress it. Either way: not our problem to engineer.

Reason 4: Subtracting PE could actually hurt

Imagine subtracting PE(pos) from the decoder output, then projecting. What you're doing is:

  1. Assuming PE remained additively present (it didn't, see Reason 1).
  2. Forcibly removing position-aware signal that the model may legitimately use (e.g., the model might want to predict end-of-sentence at higher positions).
  3. Adding an arbitrary engineering choice that the architect (you) decided was useful — even though the network could do better by learning what to do with position info itself.

You'd be imposing a prior that doesn't match how the network actually uses information. The clean abstraction — "add PE at input, let the network figure out the rest" — is more powerful precisely because it doesn't make assumptions about what happens downstream.

An analogy

Adding PE is like adding spices to a stew. After hours of simmering, the spices have permeated everything — they're not floating as identifiable specks anymore. If you tried to "remove the spice" before serving, you'd actually be removing the flavor the cooking process built. The spices were meant to shape the dish, not be filtered out at the end.

The deeper principle

In deep learning, we generally avoid imposing mid-network transformations that are "obviously correct" by human intuition. Instead, we set up the architecture, define the loss, and let gradients shape the representations.

The Transformer paper is full of "minimal-intervention" choices:

  • Add (don't concat) PE — let the network learn to use it.
  • Identity residual (no transformation on skip) — let gradients flow freely.
  • Same FFN at every position — let the FFN learn position-agnostic features.
  • No explicit "language" tokens — let the model learn language from embeddings.

The general philosophy: do the minimum needed, then trust SGD to handle the rest. Subtracting PE before the output would be doing extra work the network would actually prefer to do itself.

Part 10 — Section 4: Why Self-Attention? NEW

Now that the architecture is laid out, the paper asks a fair question: why use self-attention at all? What's wrong with RNNs and CNNs?

The paper compares the three options (self-attention, recurrent, convolutional) on three criteria, summarized in a single table — let's go through each.

The three criteria

  1. Total computational complexity per layer — how much arithmetic work?
  2. Sequential operations — how much can be parallelized? (Lower = more parallel = faster on GPUs)
  3. Maximum path length — how many steps does info need to travel between two distant positions? (Lower = easier to learn long-range dependencies)

The comparison table

Layer type Complexity per layer Sequential ops Max path length
Self-Attention $O(n^2 \cdot d)$ $O(1)$ $O(1)$ ✅
Recurrent $O(n \cdot d^2)$ $O(n)$ ❌ $O(n)$ ❌
Convolutional (kernel $k$) $O(k \cdot n \cdot d^2)$ $O(1)$ ✅ $O(\log_k n)$
Self-Attn (restricted, window $r$) $O(r \cdot n \cdot d)$ $O(1)$ ✅ $O(n/r)$

Where:

  • $n$ = sequence length (typically ~30-100 tokens for translation)
  • $d$ = representation dimension (512 in the paper)
  • $k$ = convolution kernel size
  • $r$ = neighborhood size for restricted self-attention

Criterion 1: Computational complexity per layer

Self-attention: $O(n^2 d)$. The dominant cost is computing $QK^T$, which is $n \times n$ scores, each requiring a $d$-dim dot product → $n \cdot n \cdot d$ operations.

RNN: $O(n \cdot d^2)$. Each timestep computes $W \cdot h_{t-1}$, where $W$ is $d \times d$ → $d^2$ operations per step, times $n$ steps.

The crucial comparison: $n^2 d$ vs $n d^2$

Which is bigger? Divide both by $n d$:

  • Self-attention: $n$
  • RNN: $d$

So self-attention is faster than RNN when $n < d$.

For typical translation tasks: $n \approx 50$ (sentence length), $d = 512$ (model dim). Since $50 < 512$, self-attention wins on compute. ✅

(For very long sequences — say, $n = 10000$ — RNNs would actually be cheaper. This is one motivation behind "restricted self-attention" with a local window, used by sparse-attention variants.)

Criterion 2: Sequential operations (parallelizability)

This is the killer argument for self-attention.

RNN: $O(n)$ sequential operations. To compute $h_t$, you need $h_{t-1}$, which needs $h_{t-2}$, etc. You cannot start computing position 50 until position 49 is done. No parallelism within a sequence. On a GPU, this wastes massive amounts of compute.

Self-attention: $O(1)$ sequential operations. Once you have $Q, K, V$, you compute the full $n \times n$ score matrix in one parallel matrix multiplication. Every position is processed simultaneously.

RNN compute pattern (n = 5): step 1: h_1 ←─ h_0 step 2: h_2 ←─ h_1 ← can only start after step 1 step 3: h_3 ←─ h_2 step 4: h_4 ←─ h_3 step 5: h_5 ←─ h_4 TOTAL: 5 sequential steps. GPU mostly idle. Self-attention compute pattern (n = 5): step 1: compute Q, K, V for all 5 positions (parallel) step 2: compute QK^T (one big matmul) (parallel) step 3: softmax, multiply by V (parallel) TOTAL: ~3 sequential steps, all heavily parallel matmuls. GPU saturated.

This is the reason Transformers train so much faster than RNNs of comparable accuracy. Even though self-attention has higher per-step complexity ($O(n^2 d)$ vs $O(n d^2)$), the work parallelizes onto GPU hardware, while RNNs are stuck doing one step at a time.

Criterion 3: Maximum path length (long-range dependencies)

This is about learning, not just compute. Path length is the number of network operations a signal must traverse to flow from one input position to another.

Why path length matters: gradients are multiplied along the path (chain rule). Long paths → gradients shrink (vanish) or explode → distant positions struggle to learn how they relate. Long-range dependencies become invisible to the optimizer.

The numbers

  • Self-attention: $O(1)$. Position 1 can directly attend to position 100 in a single attention step. Information flow: one hop.
  • RNN: $O(n)$. Position 1's info reaches position 100 only by being passed through 99 intermediate hidden states. Each hop dilutes/transforms the signal.
  • CNN: $O(\log_k n)$. A single conv layer connects positions within a kernel window. To connect positions $n$ apart, you stack $\log_k n$ layers (each conv doubles the receptive field if $k = 2$).
Path from position 1 to position 100: Self-Attention: pos1 ─── attention ─── pos100 (single hop, O(1)) RNN: pos1 → h_2 → h_3 → ... → h_100 (99 hops, O(n)) CNN (k=3): pos1 ─ conv ─ region1 ─ conv ─ region2 ─ ... ─ pos100 (log_3(100) ≈ 5 hops, O(log n))

Short paths = easy to learn dependencies between distant positions. This is the core architectural advantage of attention: every pair of positions has a direct path.

The big picture: why self-attention wins

Property Self-Attention RNN CNN
Compute per layer Lower (when $n < d$) Comparable Higher (factor of $k$)
Parallelism ✅ Full ❌ None ✅ Full
Long-range info flow ✅ Direct ($O(1)$) ❌ Many hops ($O(n)$) ⚠ Log hops

Self-attention wins on all three when sequences aren't too long.

Bonus argument: interpretability

The paper also notes that attention has a side benefit: you can visualize the attention weights.

After training, you can pull out the softmax weights for any input and see exactly which positions each head was focusing on. Visualizations of trained Transformers reveal heads that have learned grammatical structures (subject-verb agreement, coreference), syntactic dependencies, and so on.

RNNs and CNNs are much more opaque — their hidden states are dense soup. Attention provides a "natural" interpretability lens.

Caveat: the quadratic memory problem

The $n^2$ in $O(n^2 d)$ is also memory: you store the $n \times n$ attention matrix. For long sequences (e.g., $n = 10000$), this becomes prohibitive. Modern variants — sparse attention, linear attention, FlashAttention, state-space models like Mamba — try to address this. But for the sequence lengths the paper deals with (translation: tens to low hundreds of tokens), $n^2$ is fine.

Mental model

Self-attention wins three battles at once: cheaper (when sequences are short relative to model dim), fully parallel (matches modern GPU hardware perfectly), direct long-range connections (gradients don't have to crawl through dozens of intermediate states).

RNNs lose on parallelism and path length. CNNs lose on path length. Self-attention takes the crown.

Part 11 — Section 5.1: Training Data and Batching NEW

Section 5 of the paper covers training details — data, hardware, optimizer, regularization. These are mostly engineering choices, but a few are worth understanding because they reveal practical Transformer tricks.

Let's start with 5.1: how the data was prepared and batched.

1. The datasets

Dataset Size Tokenization
WMT 2014 English-German ~4.5M sentence pairs Byte-pair encoding (BPE), shared source-target vocab of ~37k tokens
WMT 2014 English-French ~36M sentence pairs Word-piece encoding, 32k token vocab

These are standard machine-translation benchmark datasets. WMT = "Workshop on Machine Translation" — a yearly competition with curated parallel text.

2. What is Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)?

You can't just use whole words as your vocabulary — the model would never see rare words during training, and the vocab would be huge (millions of words). You also can't use raw characters — the sequences become too long.

BPE is a middle ground. It builds a vocabulary of subwords:

  1. Start with all single characters as your vocabulary.
  2. Find the most frequent adjacent pair of tokens in the training data.
  3. Merge that pair into a new token, add it to the vocabulary.
  4. Repeat until you have, say, 37k tokens.

Result: common words become single tokens; rare words get broken into recognizable pieces.

Word BPE tokens ───────────── ────────────────── "the" ["the"] ← common, one token "running" ["run", "ning"] ← decomposed into pieces "antidisestab- ["anti", "dis", "estab", "lish", "ment", "arian", "ism"] lishmentariani- ← long rare word, many pieces sm"

Why this matters: an unknown word like "antidisestablishmentarianism" can still be processed because its pieces ("anti", "estab", etc.) are familiar.

3. Shared source-target vocabulary

For En-De, they use a single vocabulary for both source (English) and target (German). Why?

  • Many tokens are shared (numbers, proper nouns, punctuation, common subwords).
  • Lets them tie the input embedding, output embedding, and pre-softmax linear — all three using the same ~37k × 512 matrix.
  • If source/target had separate vocabs, you'd need separate embedding matrices, breaking the weight-tying trick from Section 3.4.

This is the practical reason "shared embedding" works: when source and target are linguistically similar (both Indo-European, both using Latin alphabet, lots of shared symbols), one vocab handles both fine.

4. Batching by length

Here's a clever data-engineering detail:

"Sentence pairs were batched together by approximate sequence length."

Why? Because of padding waste.

The padding problem

Transformers process fixed-size tensors. If a batch contains sentences of lengths $[5, 30, 50, 8]$, you must pad them all to length 50 (the longest in the batch). The padding tokens consume compute but contribute nothing to the loss.

Bad batching (random sentences): Sentence A (len 5): [A1 A2 A3 A4 A5 _ _ _ ... _ ] 30 padding tokens Sentence B (len 30): [B1 ............... B30 _ ... _] 20 padding tokens Sentence C (len 50): [C1 ........................ C50] 0 padding tokens Sentence D (len 8): [D1 ......... D8 _ _ _ ...... _] 42 padding tokens ↑ Tons of wasted compute. Good batching (similar lengths together): Batch 1 (lengths 5-8): short sentences together, padding to ~10 Batch 2 (lengths 25-30): medium sentences together, padding to ~30 Batch 3 (lengths 45-50): long sentences together, padding to ~50 Each batch has minimal padding overhead. Much more efficient.

Length-bucketed batching is a standard NLP trick — same training data, but ~2-5× less wasted compute.

5. Batch size: ~25,000 tokens per batch

The paper says:

"Each training batch contained a set of sentence pairs containing approximately 25,000 source tokens and 25,000 target tokens."

Note: batch size is measured in tokens, not sentences. If sentences average 30 tokens, that's ~833 sentence pairs per batch. If they average 50 tokens, ~500 pairs.

Why measure in tokens? Because the compute per batch depends on total tokens (via $n^2$ attention), not on number of sentences. Using tokens gives consistent compute across batches with different sentence lengths.

Mental model

Training data prep boils down to three good engineering decisions: (1) use subword tokenization so rare words don't kill you, (2) share the vocabulary across source and target so weight tying works, (3) batch sentences of similar length together so you don't waste compute on padding.

Section 5.2 — Hardware and Schedule

Quick numbers:

  • Hardware: 8 NVIDIA P100 GPUs (high-end for 2017; ~21 TFLOPS per GPU)
  • Base model: ~0.4 seconds per training step, trained for 100,000 steps → about 12 hours total.
  • Big model: ~1.0 seconds per step, trained for 300,000 steps → about 3.5 days.

For 2017, this was remarkably fast. Prior state-of-the-art neural translation models trained for weeks on more hardware. The combination of full parallelism within each batch (no RNN bottleneck) and good hardware utilization let Transformers train ~10-100× faster than comparable RNN models.

The result: a state-of-the-art model trained overnight, not over weeks. This was a huge deal.

Section 5.3 — Optimizer

The paper uses Adam, a variant of SGD that adapts the learning rate per parameter using running averages of recent gradients.

Hyperparameters:

  • $\beta_1 = 0.9$ — momentum (smoothing recent gradient direction)
  • $\beta_2 = 0.98$ — slightly higher than Adam's default 0.999 (longer averaging for the variance estimate)
  • $\epsilon = 10^{-9}$ — numerical stability

(If you're not deeply familiar with Adam, the rough mental model is: "SGD that automatically picks a per-parameter learning rate based on how 'wiggly' each parameter's gradient has been recently.")

The learning rate schedule — this is the unusual part

The paper introduces a specific schedule:

$$\text{lrate} = d_{\text{model}}^{-0.5} \cdot \min\!\left(\text{step}^{-0.5},\; \text{step} \cdot \text{warmup}^{-1.5}\right)$$

With $\text{warmup} = 4000$. Let's decode what this does in two phases.

Phase 1 — Warmup (step < 4000): linear increase

When step is small, $\text{step}^{-0.5}$ (which is large for small step) is bigger than $\text{step} \cdot \text{warmup}^{-1.5}$. So the $\min$ picks the second term:

$$\text{lrate} = d_{\text{model}}^{-0.5} \cdot \text{step} \cdot \text{warmup}^{-1.5}$$

This is linear in step: the lr grows from 0 to its peak value, reaching the max at step = warmup_steps.

Phase 2 — Decay (step > 4000): inverse-square-root falloff

After warmup, $\text{step}^{-0.5}$ becomes the smaller term:

$$\text{lrate} = d_{\text{model}}^{-0.5} \cdot \text{step}^{-0.5}$$

The lr now decays as $1/\sqrt{\text{step}}$ — slow, gentle decrease over the rest of training.

Visualizing the schedule

lrate ↑ │ ╱╲ │ ╱ ╲___ │ ╱ ╲___ │ ╱ ╲____ │╱ ╲_____ │ ╲________ │ ╲____ │ └────────────────────────────────────────────→ step 0 4000 (warmup) 100k ←warmup→ ←──── inverse sqrt decay ────→

Why does Transformer training need warmup?

Empirically, Transformers trained from scratch with a high learning rate at step 0 often diverge — the loss blows up. Reasons:

  • LayerNorm and softmax interact awkwardly with large random initial weights. Until the model finds a stable region of the loss landscape, big updates can push it into a regime where gradients explode.
  • The attention softmax is particularly sensitive: bad initial attention patterns can produce huge gradients.
  • Adam's running estimates of gradient mean/variance are unreliable in the first few steps (they need data to stabilize).

Warmup lets the model "settle in" — small steps initially, gradually ramping up as the model finds a workable region. This is now a standard practice for any Transformer training.

Section 5.4 — Regularization

The paper uses three regularization techniques.

(a) Dropout — $P_{\text{drop}} = 0.1$

What it is: During training, randomly set some activations to 0 with probability $p$. At inference, use all activations (scaled appropriately).

Before dropout: [0.3, 0.8, -0.2, 0.5, 0.9, -0.4] ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ random mask: [1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 1] ← 0 with prob 0.1 ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ After dropout: [0.3, 0, -0.2, 0.5, 0, -0.4] ^ ^ zeroed out zeroed out

Intuition: the model can't rely on any single neuron — it has to learn redundant, robust features. This acts as a stochastic ensemble: each training step uses a different "sub-network" by dropping different neurons. Empirically, dropout reduces overfitting.

Where applied in the Transformer:

  • To the output of each sub-layer, before it's added to the residual and normalized.
  • To the embedding + positional encoding sum, before feeding into the encoder/decoder.

So at every sub-layer, after computing the sub-layer's output but before the "+residual" step, 10% of the activations get zeroed.

(b) Label Smoothing — $\epsilon_{\text{ls}} = 0.1$

This one is more subtle but has a big effect.

The problem: normal cross-entropy loss uses "hard" one-hot targets. For target token "cat" out of vocabulary V = 37,000:

$$\text{target} = [0, 0, \dots, 1, \dots, 0]$$

(One at the "cat" position, zeros elsewhere.) This trains the model to output probability $\to 1$ for "cat" and $\to 0$ for everything else.

The model has to make its logit for "cat" enormously large compared to the rest. To do this, it pushes logits to extreme values — but extreme outputs:

  1. Make the model overconfident, even when wrong.
  2. Make training unstable (large logits → large gradients).

The fix: use "soft" targets. Replace the one-hot with a distribution that has $1 - \epsilon$ for the correct token and $\epsilon / (V - 1)$ smeared across the rest:

$$\text{target}_{\text{smoothed}} = [\frac{0.1}{V-1}, \frac{0.1}{V-1}, \dots, 0.9, \dots, \frac{0.1}{V-1}]$$

(For $\epsilon_{\text{ls}} = 0.1$: the correct token gets 0.9, every other token gets a tiny ~$0.0000027$.)

Why this works

  1. Prevents overconfidence. The model never needs to push correct-token probability above 0.9 — there's no reward beyond that.
  2. Better generalization. A less-confident model handles uncertain cases more gracefully.
  3. Improves BLEU. The paper notes a counterintuitive result: label smoothing hurts perplexity (the model is technically less confident on the correct answer) but improves accuracy and BLEU (it makes fewer catastrophic mistakes).

(c) Implicit regularization (mentioned previously)

Weight tying ($W_{\text{out}} = E^T$, from Section 3.4) also acts as regularization. Sharing the matrix across input/output is a strong constraint that improves generalization.

Mental model for Section 5

The Transformer's training "recipe" is the same one most modern models still use: Adam with warmup + inverse-sqrt decay, dropout on sub-layer outputs, and label smoothing on the targets. Three ingredients. Warmup prevents early divergence; dropout prevents overfitting through neuron-level robustness; label smoothing prevents overconfidence and improves generalization.
