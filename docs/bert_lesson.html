Orientation — where BERT sits in the 2018 timeline

You already know GPT-1 cold. So the cleanest way to teach BERT is as a series of deliberate departures from GPT-1's recipe — each one defended with a specific argument. That's how this lesson is organized: every BERT design choice gets compared to its GPT-1 counterpart and we ask why this and not that.

Part 1 — Section 1 (Introduction): the bidirectionality argument

The two strategies the field had settled into

By late 2018, two ways to use language model pre-training had emerged:

(a) Feature-based. Pre-train a model. Freeze it. Extract its hidden states as fixed features and feed them into a task-specific architecture trained from scratch on labeled data. Example: ELMo.

(b) Fine-tuning. Pre-train a model. Add a small task-specific head. Train everything (pre-trained weights + head) end-to-end on labeled data. The architecture is identical across tasks. Example: GPT-1.

BERT belongs to the fine-tuning camp. The paper's first major claim is that fine-tuning is the better paradigm — because it lets you reuse not just the representations but the whole network's computation, with minimal task-specific engineering. You already accepted this argument when you read GPT-1.

The directionality limitation

Here's the new wrinkle BERT adds: existing fine-tuning approaches are unidirectional, and that's a serious bottleneck.

GPT-1 uses a left-to-right Transformer decoder. At every position $i$, the model can attend to positions $1, \ldots, i$ but not to $i+1, \ldots, n$. This is fine — necessary, even — if your goal is generation. But for understanding, it's an arbitrary constraint. Consider:

"The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too large."

To resolve "it" (the trophy), you need to look forward to "was too large" and reason that suitcases being large isn't the issue. A left-to-right model has access to that information only after "it" has already been processed; its representation of "it" at the moment of prediction can't depend on the future.

For token-level tasks like named entity recognition (NER) or extractive QA — where the decision about token $i$ should depend on what surrounds it on both sides — this hurts a lot. The paper's quantitative claim: bidirectional pre-training is what closes the gap on these tasks.

Why didn't GPT-1 just go bidirectional, then?

Because of a fundamental problem: you can't train a bidirectional language model directly under the standard "predict the next token" objective.

Recall the LM objective: $$L \;=\; \sum_i \log P(u_i \mid u_1, \ldots, u_{i-1}; \Theta).$$ If you let the model attend bidirectionally (no causal mask), then when computing $P(u_i \mid \text{context})$, the model can attend to position $i$ itself in some layer through some chain of attention links. After even a single self-attention layer, every position's representation contains information about every other position — including its own identity. The "prediction" becomes trivial: look at yourself, copy yourself, output yourself. The model achieves zero loss while learning nothing.

This is the "see itself" problem. It's why GPT-1 had to use a causal mask: to prevent any leakage of $u_i$ into the representation at position $i$.

BERT's escape from the see-itself problem: masked language modeling. Don't ask the model to predict every token. Instead, randomly replace some tokens with a special [MASK] symbol, and ask the model to predict only the original identities of the masked positions. Since the input no longer contains the answer at the masked positions, the model can safely attend bidirectionally — there's nothing to "see" at the masked spot but the mask symbol itself. We'll formalize this in Part 5; for now, hold onto the intuition that masking removes the information that would otherwise leak through bidirectional attention.

What the paper claims to contribute

  1. Bidirectional pre-training matters. Demonstrated by ablating against a left-to-right baseline that's otherwise identical to BERT.
  2. One unified architecture beats task-specific architectures. Same idea as GPT-1, but pushed harder — BERT is SOTA on 11 different tasks with essentially no per-task architecture changes.
  3. Scale + masked LM transfers extraordinarily well. BERT-large (340M params) sets new SOTA on basically every benchmark they try.
Mental model after Section 1:
  • Two fine-tuning-paradigm extensions over GPT-1: (a) bidirectional attention, (b) masked-LM objective that enables (a).
  • The "see itself" problem is what forces the masking trick — bidirectional + raw LM objective doesn't work.
  • Same fine-tune-end-to-end philosophy as GPT-1: one architecture, minimal head per task, all weights updated.

Doubt anticipated: why not predict every token bidirectionally using a separate forward pass per position?

You could, in principle: for each position $i$, hide $u_i$, run a bidirectional Transformer over the rest, predict $u_i$. But that's $n$ forward passes per training sequence — quadratically wasteful compared to GPT's single pass that predicts all $n$ tokens at once. BERT's MLM is the efficient amortized version: predict ~15% of positions per forward pass, accept that you train on less signal per pass, but parallelize fully. The trade is "see less per pass, but the per-pass signal is now bidirectional".

Doubt anticipated: why a single bidirectional model and not ELMo's "two unidirectional LMs glued together"?

ELMo trains a left-to-right LSTM LM and a right-to-left LSTM LM independently, then concatenates their hidden states. Each direction is shallow in its conditioning — at position $i$, the L→R model sees only the left context and the R→L model sees only the right context. They're combined after the fact. That's strictly weaker than a model where every layer can attend in both directions: in BERT, at every layer, the representation of position $i$ is a function of both left and right context simultaneously, and the layers above can build on that. ELMo's combination is a "shallow concatenation of two unidirectional views"; BERT's bidirectionality is "deep simultaneous integration".

Doubt anticipated: is "bidirectional encoder" not just a rebranding of "encoder"?

Yes, in the architecture sense. A Transformer encoder (as in Vaswani 2017) has no causal mask — every position attends to every other position. So "BERT is an encoder" and "BERT is bidirectional" describe the same thing. The reason the paper uses "bidirectional" throughout is to draw the contrast with GPT-1 ("unidirectional") rather than with translation models ("encoder-decoder"). Different rhetorical framing of the same architectural fact.

Part 3 — Section 3.1 (Model Architecture)

BERT's architecture is a stack of standard Transformer encoder blocks. Nothing exotic. The only thing to know:

Doubt anticipated: BERT-base is literally identical in scale to GPT-1 (12 / 768 / 12). Coincidence?

Deliberate. The paper explicitly states: "BERT-base was chosen to have the same model size as OpenAI GPT for comparison purposes." It's an apples-to-apples controlled experiment. Anything BERT-base wins by must come from the training procedure (bidirectional, MLM, NSP, different corpus), not from extra capacity. That's a clean ablation design — if you didn't fix the architecture, you couldn't disentangle "bidirectional helps" from "more parameters helps". This is one of those small methodological details that makes the paper's comparisons honest.

Inside an encoder block (refresher)

Each of the $L$ blocks does, in order:

  1. Multi-head self-attention (no causal mask — every position can attend to every other).
  2. Add residual + LayerNorm.
  3. Position-wise feed-forward: two linear layers with a GELU activation in between, inner dim $4H$.
  4. Add residual + LayerNorm.

Same as Vaswani's encoder. The only minor change: BERT uses GELU (Gaussian Error Linear Unit) for the feed-forward activation rather than ReLU. Smoother gradient profile near zero; small but consistent improvement empirically. Not load-bearing theoretically.

Part 4 — Section 3.2 (Input/Output Representations)

This section is where most of BERT's "engineering moves" live: the special tokens, the segment embeddings, the WordPiece vocabulary. Each one is small in isolation but together they give BERT the flexibility to handle single sentences and sentence pairs through a uniform interface.

The input format, in one sentence

Every input to BERT is a sequence that starts with a special [CLS] token, contains one or two text segments separated by a [SEP] token, and ends with another [SEP]. Concretely, for a sentence pair $(A, B)$:

[CLS] tok_A_1 tok_A_2 ... tok_A_m [SEP] tok_B_1 tok_B_2 ... tok_B_n [SEP]

For a single sentence, just drop $B$ — keep the first [SEP].

Doubt anticipated: why segment embeddings? Couldn't [SEP] already mark the boundary?

The [SEP] token marks where the boundary is at the token level, but nothing else about the embedding tells a position whether it belongs to $A$ or $B$. Without segment embeddings, positions 6 and 7 in the example above would have no signal saying "you are part of segment $B$" — only the cumulative knowledge that they come after a [SEP]. The attention layers would have to learn to count [SEP]s to figure out membership.

Adding a constant $\mathbf{e}_A$ to every position in $A$ and a constant $\mathbf{e}_B$ to every position in $B$ makes segment membership a direct additive feature available at every layer. Much easier for the model to use, and only 2 extra vectors of parameters. A small, principled inductive bias.

(There are only two segment embeddings, $\mathbf{e}_A$ and $\mathbf{e}_B$. Single-sentence inputs use $\mathbf{e}_A$ throughout.)

Doubt anticipated: why [CLS]? Why not just use the last token like GPT-1?

This is one of the cleanest consequences of bidirectionality.

In GPT-1, the model is causal: position $i$'s hidden state depends on positions $1, \ldots, i$. The only position whose hidden state is a function of the entire input is the last one. So GPT-1 must use the last position for sequence-level tasks; there's no choice.

In BERT, the model is bidirectional: every position's hidden state depends on every other position, including itself. Every position sees the full input. So "which position do we use for classification?" becomes an open question. The paper's answer: prepend a dedicated token ([CLS]) whose hidden state is trained to be a sequence-level summary. The MLM objective doesn't touch [CLS]; the NSP objective trains it to encode the relationship between segments $A$ and $B$. So [CLS] becomes the designated "summary register".

Why dedicate a special token rather than (say) averaging all hidden states? Two reasons: (1) averaging treats every position equally, but in practice some positions are more informative than others — better to let the model learn the weighting, which is what the attention into [CLS] effectively does; (2) a special token gives a stable position-independent "address" for the classifier head to read from, simplifying the software interface.

WordPiece tokenization

BERT uses WordPiece with a 30,000-token vocabulary. WordPiece is a subword tokenization algorithm closely related to BPE (which GPT-1 used). Both split rare words into smaller pieces; the differences are in the exact merging criterion (WordPiece maximizes the likelihood of the corpus under a unigram language model; BPE greedily merges most-frequent pairs).

Doubt anticipated: why WordPiece over BPE?

Honestly, the choice is mostly historical/pragmatic: WordPiece was the in-house Google tokenizer (used for machine translation) and BERT inherited it. Performance differences between WordPiece and BPE at comparable vocab sizes are small; subsequent models have used either. The mechanical effect is the same: convert any English text into a stream of subword units from a fixed 30k vocabulary, with no out-of-vocabulary symbols.

One small visible artifact: WordPiece marks word-internal continuations with ##. So "playing" might tokenize as play + ##ing. The ## prefix tells you "this piece attaches to the previous one without a space". Cosmetic, but useful to know if you're ever debugging BERT outputs.

Output side

For every input position $i$, the final encoder layer produces a hidden state $\mathbf{h}_i \in \mathbb{R}^H$. Two outputs are then read off:

  • $C \in \mathbb{R}^H$: the hidden state at the [CLS] position. Used for sequence-level tasks (NSP, sentence classification).
  • $T_i \in \mathbb{R}^H$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$: the hidden states at every other position. Used for token-level tasks (MLM, NER, span prediction).

Important: these aren't separate model outputs. They're just the same vector $\mathbf{h}_i$ at different positions, renamed for clarity depending on whether you're talking about the sequence-level or token-level head. There's one network, one forward pass, two ways to read its output.

Part 5 — Section 3.3 (Pre-training): Masked LM + Next Sentence Prediction

Pre-training BERT uses two objectives simultaneously, summed:

$$L_{\text{pretrain}} \;=\; L_{\text{MLM}} \;+\; L_{\text{NSP}}.$$

We'll take them in turn. MLM is the deep one; NSP is simpler but worth examining closely because it turns out (in hindsight) to be the more controversial of the two.

3.3.1 — Masked Language Modeling (MLM)

The procedure

Take a sequence of tokens after WordPiece tokenization. Randomly select 15% of token positions (excluding [CLS] and [SEP]). At each selected position, the token is replaced according to the following rule:

The 80/10/10 corruption rule for each of the 15% selected positions:
  • 80% of the time, replace the token with [MASK].
  • 10% of the time, replace the token with a random token from the vocabulary.
  • 10% of the time, leave the token unchanged.
The model then computes hidden states for the whole sequence, and at each of the originally-selected positions, predicts the original token via a softmax over the vocabulary.

Let $M \subset \{1, \ldots, n\}$ be the set of masked positions. Let $\mathbf{x}$ be the original token sequence and $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ the corrupted version that goes into BERT. The MLM loss is:

$$L_{\text{MLM}} \;=\; -\sum_{i \in M} \log P(x_i \mid \tilde{\mathbf{x}}; \Theta) \;=\; -\sum_{i \in M} \log \operatorname{softmax}\bigl(\mathbf{h}_i \, W_e^\top\bigr)_{x_i}.$$

where $\mathbf{h}_i$ is the final hidden state at position $i$ and $W_e$ is the input embedding matrix (tied — same trick as GPT-1 and Vaswani).

Why 15%?

This is the number to defend. Three forces are in tension:

The mask rate trade-off:
  • Too low (say 5%): you get fewer prediction targets per sequence, so you waste compute — each forward pass produces useful loss signal at only a handful of positions. Training is slow.
  • Too high (say 50% or 90%): you destroy too much of the input context. The remaining unmasked tokens can't supply enough bidirectional signal to predict the masked ones, and the task becomes nearly impossible. Also, the gap between pre-training (heavy masking) and fine-tuning (no masking) grows wider.
  • 15% is a pragmatic middle: enough prediction targets to learn from, little enough corruption that context remains informative.
The paper doesn't sweep this hyperparameter in detail; 15% was inherited from prior work on denoising autoencoders and worked well enough that nobody questioned it for a while. Later work (especially the "Should You Mask 15% in MLM?" paper, Wettig et al. 2022) showed that higher rates (up to 40%) work fine and sometimes better when the model is large enough. But 15% has stuck as the canonical number.

Why the 80/10/10 mix?

This is the single most subtle design choice in the paper, and the one most worth internalizing. Each of the three branches solves a specific problem.

The 80% [MASK] branch: This is the "real" prediction task. Most of the time, the corruption is to replace the token with [MASK], and the model has to predict what was there. This is the core MLM signal.

The problem this alone would create: [MASK] is a token that appears only during pre-training. At fine-tuning time, the model never sees [MASK]. So if 100% of MLM prediction came from [MASK]-replaced positions, the model would learn a strategy specialized to [MASK]-shaped holes, and that strategy would not transfer to downstream tasks where every position has a real token. There would be a distributional mismatch — a "pre-train / fine-tune skew".

The 10% random branch: Replace with a random vocabulary token. Now the "corrupted" position contains a real (but wrong) token — same surface form as fine-tuning. This forces the model to learn that it cannot fully trust the token at any position — the model should use context to either confirm or override what's there. This builds the right inductive bias: every position's representation should be a smoothed/contextualized version of its identity, not just a lookup.

The 10% unchanged branch: Leave the token as is, but still predict it. This sounds weird — the answer is sitting right there in the input. But it's important precisely because the model doesn't know which positions are unchanged. It has to predict every selected position, and it can't tell whether the visible token is original or random. So it can't just "copy the token through" — it has to use context anyway. The unchanged branch also stays close to fine-tuning distribution (real token, no mask).

The net effect: The model is forced to compute a meaningful contextual representation at every position regardless of which corruption was applied, because at inference it can't see which positions were chosen. This makes BERT's representations useful for every fine-tuning task — not just for the artificial "predict the masked token" game.

Doubt anticipated: in the 10% random case, isn't the model trained to predict a token when the input is a different random token? Doesn't that inject noise?

Yes, it does inject some noise. But the random tokens are uniformly drawn from the vocab, which means most of them are unrelated to the surrounding context. The signal from context overwhelmingly outweighs the misleading signal from the random token, especially because the model has access to the full bidirectional context. The cost is small (~1.5% of all tokens are random replacements, since 10% of 15% = 1.5%). The benefit — closing the pre-train / fine-tune distribution gap — is large. It's a worthwhile trade.

Doubt anticipated: the loss is computed over only 15% of positions. Isn't that a massive waste of compute?

Yes — and this is one of BERT's legitimate weaknesses. Compare to GPT-style left-to-right LM, where every token contributes a loss term. BERT does the same amount of forward-pass work but extracts 1/6.7th the gradient signal per pass. RoBERTa later mitigates this by training for much longer; ELECTRA (Clark et al., 2020) replaces MLM with a "replaced-token-detection" objective that uses every position. The 15%-only-loss design is a real inefficiency baked into BERT.

3.3.2 — Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)

The procedure

For each pre-training example, construct a sentence pair $(A, B)$:

  • 50% of the time, $B$ is the actual sentence that follows $A$ in the source document. Label: IsNext.
  • 50% of the time, $B$ is a random sentence drawn from elsewhere in the corpus. Label: NotNext.

Feed the full sequence [CLS] A [SEP] B [SEP] through BERT. Take the [CLS] hidden state $C \in \mathbb{R}^H$. Project to 2 logits via a small classifier $W_{\text{NSP}} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times 2}$. Cross-entropy against the binary label.

$$L_{\text{NSP}} \;=\; -\log P(\text{label} \mid C; W_{\text{NSP}}) \;=\; -\log \operatorname{softmax}(C \, W_{\text{NSP}})_{\text{label}}.$$

Why NSP at all?

The motivating concern: MLM trains the model to predict tokens within a sentence, but many downstream tasks (entailment, QA, sentence pair similarity) require reasoning about relationships between sentences. NSP is supposed to inject that capability into pre-training by forcing [CLS] to encode whether $A$ and $B$ are coherent neighbors.

It's also nearly free: NSP examples come from the same corpus you're already using for MLM, and you can compute both losses in the same forward pass.

Doubt anticipated (historical context): does NSP actually help?

At the time of publication, the BERT paper's ablations (we'll see them in Part 8) showed small but consistent gains from NSP. Subsequent work — most clearly RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) — showed that NSP doesn't help, and can even hurt, once you control for other factors (more training, more data, longer sequences). The current consensus is that NSP is too easy: distinguishing "next sentence" from "random sentence" mostly amounts to topic detection (are these two sentences about the same thing?), which the model can solve trivially from word-overlap statistics, and which doesn't actually teach useful sentence-relationship reasoning.

So NSP is a historically interesting design that turned out to be wrong. We'll dwell on this more in Part 8 (ablations) and in the weaknesses section at the end. For now, accept that BERT-the-original-paper trained with NSP, and notice the contrast to how we'd build it today.

Putting MLM and NSP together

The combined pre-training loss is:

$$L_{\text{pretrain}} \;=\; L_{\text{MLM}} \;+\; L_{\text{NSP}}.$$

One forward pass through BERT produces both: the final hidden state at [CLS] feeds the NSP head; the final hidden states at masked positions feed the MLM head; one backward pass through both heads.

Pre-training corpus

BERT is pre-trained on the concatenation of:

  • BooksCorpus — ~800M words (same as GPT-1).
  • English Wikipedia — ~2,500M words (text only, no lists/tables).

Total: ~3.3B words.

Doubt anticipated: why these two corpora and not, say, web crawl?

The paper explicitly notes that "it is critical to use a document-level corpus rather than a shuffled sentence-level corpus" to learn long-range dependencies. Both BooksCorpus and Wikipedia have document structure: books have chapter-level coherence, Wikipedia articles have paragraph-level argumentative structure. By contrast, the typical web crawl of the day was lower quality and noisier. The choice mirrors GPT-1's reasoning (recall: GPT-1 explicitly preferred BooksCorpus over the 1B Word Benchmark for the same reason).

A later paper, RoBERTa, showed that BERT was actually under-trained on data: adding more (CC-News, OpenWebText, Stories) gave large improvements. So the corpus choice was defensible but conservative.

Training hyperparameters (briefly)

  • Batch size: 256 sequences × 512 tokens = 128,000 tokens per batch.
  • Steps: 1,000,000 (~40 epochs over the 3.3B word corpus).
  • Optimizer: Adam, learning rate $10^{-4}$, linear warmup for first 10k steps.
  • Weight decay 0.01, dropout 0.1.
  • GELU activation in the FFN.
  • BERT-base trained for 4 days on 16 TPU chips; BERT-large for 4 days on 64 TPU chips.

Doubt anticipated: why learned absolute positional embeddings rather than sinusoidal?

Same answer as GPT-1: empirically equivalent up to the training context length, simpler to implement, and 512 is well within the trainable range. The cost — no extrapolation to longer sequences than were seen during training — wasn't a concern at the time. Later models (T5, RoPE-based, ALiBi) revisited this once long-context inference became a focus.

Part 6 — Section 3.4 (Fine-tuning BERT)

Once pre-training is done, fine-tuning is straightforward: take BERT, add a tiny task-specific head, train end-to-end on labeled data for a few epochs (typically 2–4).

The interesting part is how the same network is adapted to four different task shapes. Figure 4 in the paper diagrams them; we'll go through each.

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ (a) SENTENCE-PAIR CLASSIFICATION (MNLI, QQP, QNLI, STS-B, MRPC, RTE) │ │ │ │ Input: [CLS] sentence_A [SEP] sentence_B [SEP] │ │ Head: classifier on h_[CLS] → softmax over K classes │ │ Loss: cross-entropy on label │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ (b) SINGLE-SENTENCE CLASSIFICATION (SST-2, CoLA) │ │ │ │ Input: [CLS] sentence [SEP] │ │ Head: classifier on h_[CLS] → softmax over K classes │ │ Loss: cross-entropy on label │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ (c) QUESTION ANSWERING / SPAN PREDICTION (SQuAD v1.1, v2.0) │ │ │ │ Input: [CLS] question [SEP] passage [SEP] │ │ Head: two vectors S, E in R^H learned during fine-tuning. │ │ For each passage position i: │ │ start_score_i = S · h_i │ │ end_score_i = E · h_i │ │ softmax independently over positions for start, for end. │ │ Loss: -log P(start) - log P(end) with gold span │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ (d) TOKEN TAGGING / NER (CoNLL-2003) │ │ │ │ Input: [CLS] sentence [SEP] │ │ Head: per-token classifier W_tag on each h_i → softmax over tags │ │ Loss: sum of per-token cross-entropies │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

The classification head, in math

For sentence-level tasks (a, b), the head is a single linear layer applied to the [CLS] hidden state $C$:

$$P(y \mid x) \;=\; \operatorname{softmax}(C \, W_{\text{cls}}), \qquad W_{\text{cls}} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times K}.$$

This is the only new parameter — just $H \times K$ scalars, often a few thousand parameters. Everything else (the 110M or 340M weights of BERT) is initialized from pre-training and then fine-tuned.

Span prediction for SQuAD, in math

SQuAD is the canonical extractive QA benchmark: given a question and a passage, find the contiguous span of passage tokens that answers the question. So the output is two indices: start and end.

BERT introduces two new vectors $\mathbf{S}, \mathbf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^H$ during fine-tuning. For each token $i$ in the passage, the model computes:

$$\text{start score: } s_i = \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{h}_i, \qquad \text{end score: } e_i = \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{h}_i.$$

These are then softmax-normalized independently over the passage positions:

$$P(\text{start} = i) = \frac{\exp(s_i)}{\sum_j \exp(s_j)}, \qquad P(\text{end} = i) = \frac{\exp(e_i)}{\sum_j \exp(e_j)}.$$

The training loss is the sum of two negative log-likelihoods (one for the gold start position, one for the gold end position):

$$L_{\text{SQuAD}} \;=\; -\log P(\text{start} = i^*) - \log P(\text{end} = j^*).$$

Doubt anticipated: why model start and end independently rather than jointly?

This is exactly the question to ask. Predicting start and end jointly would mean modeling $P(\text{start}, \text{end})$ directly — a distribution over $O(n^2)$ span candidates per example. That's expensive, and parametrizing it requires more head parameters.

The independence assumption $P(\text{start}, \text{end}) \approx P(\text{start}) \cdot P(\text{end})$ is a modeling simplification. At inference, BERT recovers a joint prediction by enumerating all valid (start, end) pairs with $\text{start} \le \text{end}$ and picking the one with the highest sum of scores $s_i + e_j$. This is cheap ($O(n^2)$ addition, but no extra forward passes) and works well in practice.

Why does independence work? Because the bidirectional encoder has already done most of the joint reasoning inside the network. By the final layer, each $\mathbf{h}_i$ is a function of the entire sequence, including which tokens form coherent spans. The "independent" softmaxes at the head are really just two readout heads on top of a representation that already encodes span structure. The independence assumption applies only at the head, not in the deep representation.

SQuAD v2.0: handling unanswerable questions

SQuAD v2.0 adds questions that have no answer in the passage. BERT handles this by treating the [CLS] position as a "no-answer" span: train the model to predict start = end = position of [CLS] for unanswerable examples. At inference, compare the best-non-CLS-span score against the [CLS]-span score with a threshold; output "no answer" if CLS wins.

Why so few new parameters per task?

Notice how small each head is. Sentence classification: $H \times K$ params (a few thousand). NER: $H \times \text{num\_tags}$ (similar). SQuAD: $2H$ (just two vectors). Compare to BERT-base's 110M weights. The vast majority of the modeling capacity comes from pre-training; fine-tuning is essentially steering a well-developed representation toward a specific decision boundary.

Fine-tuning hyperparameters that worked across all tasks:
  • Batch size 16 or 32.
  • Learning rate: $5 \times 10^{-5}$, $3 \times 10^{-5}$, or $2 \times 10^{-5}$ (small grid search per task).
  • Number of epochs: 2, 3, or 4.
  • Same Adam, same dropout 0.1.
The fact that one hyperparameter region works across 11 different downstream tasks is itself a non-obvious empirical finding. It says the pre-trained representation is strikingly robust.

Part 7 — Section 4 (Experiments): GLUE, SQuAD, SWAG

The experiments section is long on numbers but light on conceptual content. We'll extract the patterns.

4.1 — GLUE

GLUE (General Language Understanding Evaluation) is a basket of 9 sentence/sentence-pair classification tasks: linguistic acceptability (CoLA), sentiment (SST-2), paraphrase (MRPC, QQP), similarity (STS-B), entailment (MNLI, QNLI, RTE, WNLI). Each task has its own training set, ranging from ~2.5k to 400k examples. The headline metric is the average score across tasks.

  1. BERT-base beats GPT-1 (which has the same architecture size) by ~4.5 points. Since the architecture is identical, this gap is attributable to bidirectionality + MLM + NSP. That's a meaningful chunk of the paper's evidence for its central claims.
  2. BERT-large beats BERT-base by ~2.5 more points. Even though many GLUE tasks have only thousands of examples, the bigger model still helps. Scale transfers even to small fine-tuning datasets — this becomes a major ablation finding in §5.2.

4.4 — SWAG

SWAG (Situations With Adversarial Generations) tests commonsense inference: given a sentence, pick which of four candidate continuations is most plausible.

BERT handles this as a 4-way scoring task. For each candidate $k$, build the sequence [CLS] context [SEP] candidate_k [SEP], get $C_k$, project to a scalar $s_k = C_k \cdot \mathbf{v}$ (where $\mathbf{v}$ is a learned vector). Softmax over the four candidates, train with cross-entropy.

Result: BERT-large beats human-level performance (86.3% vs 85.0%). The fact that a language model can outperform humans on a commonsense inference benchmark — even an adversarial one — was, at the time, surprising.

The big-picture pattern across §4: BERT sets SOTA on basically everything. On most tasks it beats prior SOTA by margins comparable to (or larger than) the gap between prior SOTA and the previous-previous SOTA. This is the kind of result that signals a paradigm shift: not a refinement of an existing recipe, but a new recipe that resets the frontier across an entire field.

Part 8 — Section 5 (Ablation Studies)

The ablations are where you separate the design choices' contributions from each other. There are three.

5.1 — Effect of pre-training tasks (do MLM and NSP each matter?)

The paper compares four variants, all at BERT-base scale, all on the same corpus:

Three things to extract:

(a) Removing NSP hurts only mildly.

Comparing rows 1 and 2: dropping NSP costs ~0.5 points on MNLI, ~3.5 on QNLI, ~0.6 on SQuAD. Small but nonzero gains from NSP at this scale. This was the paper's argument for keeping NSP. As noted earlier, RoBERTa later showed that with more training data and longer sequences, even these small NSP gains disappear or reverse. So the paper's ablation here was misleading — not deliberately, but because the experimental budget couldn't expose NSP's irrelevance.

(b) Replacing MLM with left-to-right LM is much more damaging.

Comparing rows 2 and 3: LTR loses ~1.8 on MNLI, ~9 on MRPC, ~10 on SQuAD. Big drops on sentence-pair and span tasks. The bidirectional MLM signal is doing real work — especially on tasks that require looking at a token from both sides.

(c) Even adding a BiLSTM on top of LTR doesn't recover MLM's performance.

Row 4 adds a randomly-initialized BiLSTM head on top of the LTR Transformer at fine-tuning time. This gives some bidirectional signal post-hoc, and indeed SQuAD recovers significantly (77.8 → 84.9). But it's still well below BERT (88.5). The takeaway: bidirectionality is most valuable when it's baked into pre-training, not bolted on during fine-tuning. The whole representation gets shaped by bidirectional context, not just the final layer.

This is the most theoretically satisfying ablation in the paper. It says: bidirectionality matters not because the final layer integrates two views, but because every layer during pre-training builds representations conditioned on both sides.

5.2 — Effect of model size

The paper trains a sequence of models from 3M to 340M parameters, fine-tuning each on GLUE tasks (some of which have very small training sets — MRPC has 3.7k examples). The result:

Every column improves monotonically with scale. The MRPC result is particularly striking — MRPC has only 3.7k labeled examples, yet a 340M-parameter model fine-tuned on it works better than a 5M-parameter one.

This contradicts the conventional wisdom of 2018, which held that very large models would overfit on small fine-tuning sets. The reason this turned out wrong: pre-training regularizes. A 340M-parameter model whose weights are initialized from a strong pre-training run is not a freely-fitting 340M-parameter model — it's a small perturbation of a model that already knows English. The effective capacity available for overfitting is much smaller than the raw parameter count suggests.

This was, in 2018, an early signal of what we now call the "scaling laws" — that pre-trained model capacity translates almost monotonically into downstream performance, even when the fine-tuning data is small. GPT-2, GPT-3, and the entire scale-up era took this lesson and ran with it.

5.3 — Feature-based approach with BERT

BERT is designed as a fine-tuning model. But you could use it feature-based, the way ELMo is used: freeze BERT, extract its hidden states, feed them as fixed input features to a task-specific architecture trained from scratch.

The paper tests this on CoNLL-2003 NER. They freeze BERT-large, extract token embeddings from various layers, feed them to a randomly-initialized 2-layer BiLSTM, and train just the BiLSTM.

Layers used as featuresNER F1 (dev)

Last 4 hidden layers (sum)95.5
Last 4 hidden layers (concat)96.1

The takeaway: feature-based BERT gets within 0.3 F1 of fully fine-tuned BERT. That's basically negligible. So you can use BERT either way — and that flexibility matters for practitioners with constrained compute (you can pre-compute frozen features once and train many small task-specific models on top).

It's also another argument that BERT's representations are genuinely rich, not just well-tuned to the fine-tuning objective. If pretraining alone produces features that get to within a fraction of a point of full fine-tuning, the pretraining has done its job.

View 3: the post-BERT lineage

BERT spawned a research direction. The most important descendants:

  • RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019). Same architecture as BERT. Drops NSP. Trains on much more data (~160GB) for much longer. Uses dynamic masking (re-randomize masks each epoch rather than fixing them once). Result: meaningfully better than BERT. The "no architecture change, just train better" message was influential.
  • ALBERT (Lan et al., 2019). Cross-layer parameter sharing — every encoder layer shares weights. Factorizes the embedding matrix to save params. Replaces NSP with a "sentence-order prediction" task that actually trains useful sentence-level representations. Smaller param count, comparable performance.
  • DistilBERT (Sanh et al., 2019). Knowledge distillation: train a smaller (6-layer) BERT to mimic BERT-base's outputs. 40% smaller, 60% faster, retains ~97% of BERT's performance. Practical deployment workhorse.
  • DeBERTa (He et al., 2020). Disentangled attention: separate content and position into two attention streams, combine carefully. Substantially better than BERT/RoBERTa on GLUE and SuperGLUE.
  • ELECTRA (Clark et al., 2020). Replaces MLM with a discriminator objective: a small generator network proposes replacements for 15% of tokens; BERT learns to detect which tokens were replaced. Loss applies to every position, not just 15% — much more sample-efficient than MLM.

And the more interesting divergence: the BERT-line never produced a "ChatGPT moment". The conversational, generative AI revolution went down the GPT line — the unidirectional decoder lineage. Why? Because BERT's MLM is inherently a denoising objective, not a generative one. You can't sample from BERT in a coherent left-to-right way; you can only fill in masks. Generation requires next-token prediction, which is the GPT setup.

So in retrospect, the 2018 fork was not just architectural but functional:

GPT-style pre-training optimizes for generation; BERT-style pre-training optimizes for representation. As of 2026, the generation path won the "consumer-facing AI" race, while the representation path remained dominant for retrieval, embeddings, classification, and RAG pipelines.

One-sentence summary of the paper

BERT shows that pre-training a bidirectional Transformer encoder with a masked language model objective produces representations that, when fine-tuned end-to-end, set new state-of-the-art on essentially every standard NLP task — closing the gap between feature-based and fine-tuning paradigms by combining the strengths of ELMo (bidirectionality) and GPT (end-to-end fine-tuning) in a single architecture.

Honest weaknesses (and what subsequent work fixed)

BERT is a remarkable paper but not flawless. Three honest weaknesses are worth naming explicitly, because they each got addressed by later work and the addressed-form is how the field thinks about pre-training today.

Weakness 1: NSP doesn't actually help.
BERT's ablations showed small gains from NSP, but those gains were artifacts of an under-trained baseline. RoBERTa demonstrated that with longer training and more data, removing NSP doesn't hurt — and sometimes helps. The probable reason: distinguishing the next sentence from a random sentence mostly reduces to topic similarity, which the model solves trivially without learning real sentence-relationship reasoning. ALBERT's sentence-order prediction (same topic, just reordered) is a harder task that does produce useful sentence-level signal. Modern recipes mostly drop NSP entirely.
Weakness 2: data scale was conservative.
3.3B words sounds like a lot in 2018, but RoBERTa (160GB), T5 (~750GB), and modern foundation models (multi-TB) showed that BERT was significantly under-trained on data. Many of the headline BERT results would improve simply by re-running pre-training on a larger corpus. The paper, being early in the scaling era, did not yet realize this.
Weakness 3: 15% masking is computationally wasteful.
BERT does a full forward and backward pass through L=12 or L=24 encoder layers, but extracts loss signal at only 15% of positions. The 85% of positions whose predictions aren't scored contribute compute without contributing gradient. Per token of training data processed, BERT is about 6.7× less sample-efficient than a comparable autoregressive LM that predicts every token. ELECTRA fixed this by applying its discriminator loss at every position; modern pre-training recipes often use similar dense-loss objectives.

Other things to keep in mind

  • BERT can't generate. The MLM objective trains a denoiser, not a next-token predictor. You can do iterative gibbs-style sampling from BERT but the output quality is poor compared to a real LM. If you want generation, you want GPT-style.
  • Fixed 512-token context window. BERT's learned absolute positional embeddings don't extrapolate. For longer documents you have to either chunk + aggregate or switch to a model with relative/sliding-window positions (Longformer, BigBird).
  • The [MASK] token never appears at fine-tune time. The 80/10/10 trick mitigates this but doesn't fully eliminate the pre-train/fine-tune distribution gap. ELECTRA's discriminator objective sidesteps it entirely.

Closing thought

If you internalize one thing from BERT, make it this: the paper's central technical contribution isn't the architecture — it's the training procedure, specifically the masked LM objective that enables bidirectional pre-training. Once you can train a bidirectional Transformer at all, the rest of the paper (the [CLS] head, the segment embeddings, the fine-tuning recipes) falls out as straightforward engineering. The conceptual leap is realizing that you can sidestep the "see itself" problem of bidirectional LMs by training a denoising objective instead of a next-token objective — and that the resulting representations transfer beautifully to fine-tuning. That single trick reset the SOTA on every standard NLP benchmark within a few months of publication, and the encoder-pretraining paradigm it launched still powers most production NLP systems that aren't doing generation.

The other paper from 2018, GPT-1, will, of course, turn out to be the lineage that produces ChatGPT and modern conversational AI. But BERT is the one that taught the field how to use deep pre-training for understanding. Both lineages start from Vaswani's 2017 Transformer; both rely on self-supervised pre-training; they diverge on whether the model is decoder-only and generative or encoder-only and discriminative. As of 2026, we live in a world where both lineages matter — and the BERT lesson is that those two design axes are independent and worth choosing deliberately.
