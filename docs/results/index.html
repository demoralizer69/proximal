2 Why higher scores = better replications

A quick visual sanity check that the grader's ranking matches what a human would call "closer."

The grader is per-page MAE on rendered screenshots — for each pixel, the absolute difference between target and candidate is averaged across the page, normalised to [0, 1], then aggregated across pages with a generalized power mean at p = 0.05. The power mean weighs the worst-scoring pages much more heavily than a plain average — a single poorly cloned page is enough to drag a website down, which mirrors how a reviewer would judge it.

Because the metric works directly on pixels, it captures the things humans actually care about: colour, layout, typography weight and spatial composition all show up as pixel disagreement. An obviously wrong background tone or a missing hero block immediately collapses the score, while a faithful clone with minor wording changes barely moves it.

Best trial scores , worst trial scores . The best is visually close to the target on every page — colour, layout and decoration are mostly preserved. The worst inverts the background, drops decorative shapes and rearranges sections, which is exactly the kind of failure a per-pixel metric is designed to punish.

4 Common patterns the model struggles with

Drafted from inspecting the worst-scoring trials and cross-referencing each task's temperature knobs.

1. Background-tone inversion

The single most expensive failure mode. When the target has a light background (bg_tone: light) and the clone renders dark — or vice versa — the per-pixel MAE collapses globally because every pixel of the page disagrees, not just the foreground. This is what produces the worst single trial in the whole batch: task #11's latest page at 0.293, where the model rendered the entire site on near-black despite the spec specifying a light cream background. The structure of the page was largely correct, but the inverted palette wipes out the score.

2. Missing decorative organic shapes

Many of the harder tasks have shape_organic ≥ 0.5 and container_escape ≥ 0.7 — large blobs, orbs and overlapping circles that sit behind the hero. In the worst clones these are simply dropped: the page reads as a flat bento grid with no background art. The model gets the type and the cards right but misses the decorative layer entirely. Task #11 (giant green orb and triangle behind the hero) and task #18 (organic green blobs bleeding past the page edge) both show this.

3. Flattened depth: shadows, double borders, neo-brutalist offsets

shadow_intensity has the strongest negative correlation with mean task score (r = −0.43). Tasks built around heavy drop shadows (neo-brutalist offset cards, skeuomorphic raised buttons) get reduced to flat outlined boxes in the worst trials — task #4's "Open HQ" radio operator UI is the canonical example: target cards have a sharp 6-8px hard offset shadow; worst trials render them with no shadow or a soft blur. Per-pixel MAE penalises this strongly because the shadow region's pixels are wrong in two places at once.

4. Typography contrast and outline type

Tasks with very high typography_contrast (the same page mixes 12pt body with 80pt display) tend to lose the extreme scale jumps in clones. Similarly, outline_typography (hollow stroked headings like "AMATEUR." in task #11) is often rendered as solid weight, or as a very faint outline that barely registers against the background. The text is in the right place, but the visual mass differs by a lot of pixels.

5. Image density and image content

image_density correlates negatively with score (r = −0.41). The pipeline uses no real assets — the target screenshots already render images as coloured placeholder rectangles, and the clone is expected to do the same. The failure mode is the clone using a different rectangle treatment (white background and a small icon, instead of a bold solid colour fill, or vice versa), which on image-heavy pages disagrees on a huge fraction of pixels.

6. Per-page variance: secondary pages drag the score down

The home page is almost always the best-cloned page in any trial — it's the most distinctive, most-described surface and the model focuses its effort there. Secondary pages (settings, history, category, profile) tend to be simpler in the target but also more idiosyncratic in the clone, and the power-mean aggregation with p = 0.05 is specifically designed to surface this — one bad page drags the website's score down sharply. Without the power mean (i.e. with a plain average), the strong home page would mask weak secondaries and many of these clones would score noticeably higher than they deserve to.
